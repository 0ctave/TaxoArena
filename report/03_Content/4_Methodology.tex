
\chapter{Formal Foundations}\label{ch:methodology}

This chapter formalises the statistical and algorithmic components
that TaxoArena's implementation actually executes: the directional
node model and its bias-corrected MLE, the Matryoshka embedding
schedule, the migration rule, the Dasgupta splitting oracle, the
trickle-routing softmax, the Bradley--Terry estimator and its MM
solver, the active scheduling utility, the three-level stopping
policy, and upward score aggregation.
Numerical-stability engineering that supports these computations but
is not itself a modelling choice---log-Bessel evaluation at high
dimension, the KDE bandwidth derivation, and the full Fisher
information matrix derivation---is summarised here and given in full
in Appendix~\ref{app:numerics}.

\section{Notation and Problem Formalisation}\label{sec:meth-notation}

Let $\mathcal{Q} = \{q_1, \ldots, q_N\}$ denote the corpus of
$N$ MMLU-Pro queries under consideration, each with a full-dimensional
MRL embedding $\mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{4096}$ stored at unit
norm; the concrete value of $N$ and the domain scope are specified
in Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental-design}.
At depth $\ell$, the effective representation of $q_i$ is the prefix
slice $\mathbf{x}_i^{(\ell)} \in \mathcal{S}^{d_\ell - 1}$
(Section~\ref{sec:meth-mrl}).
Each query carries a ground-truth domain label
$y_i^{\text{MMLU}} \in \{D_1, \ldots, D_{14}\}$.

The corpus is partitioned once, before construction begins, into a
disjoint construction set $\mathcal{Q}_{\text{con}}$ and an evaluation
set $\mathcal{Q}_{\text{eval}}$, with $\mathcal{Q} = \mathcal{Q}_{\text{con}}
\cup \mathcal{Q}_{\text{eval}}$ and
$\mathcal{Q}_{\text{con}} \cap \mathcal{Q}_{\text{eval}} = \emptyset$.
$\mathcal{Q}_{\text{con}}$ is the only query set visible to the
taxonomy construction pipeline (anchor seeding, migration, splitting,
topological optimisation) and to reference-informed guideline
generation.
$\mathcal{Q}_{\text{eval}}$ is reserved exclusively for held-out
routing and arena evaluation and is never inspected during
construction; this boundary is a methodological constraint on the
experimental design, not a configuration choice, and is the basis of
the answer-key-blind claim formalised in
Table~\ref{tab:info-separation}.

The anchor set $\mathcal{A} = \{D_1, \ldots, D_{14}\}$ is in
one-to-one correspondence with the 14 MMLU-Pro domains; each anchor
is a depth-0 node from which the pipeline induces a sub-tree of
internal nodes and leaves $\mathcal{L}(D_k)$, with full leaf set
$\mathcal{L} = \bigcup_k \mathcal{L}(D_k)$.
The membership map $\hat{D}(q_i)$ assigns each query to the anchor of
its primary leaf after construction.

Two query populations are defined per anchor.
The \textit{canonical partition}
$Q_{D_k}^{\text{canonical}} = \{q_i : y_i^{\text{MMLU}} = D_k\}$ is
the MMLU-Pro label partition; the \textit{adapted partition}
$Q_{D_k}^{\text{adapted}} = \{q_i : \hat{D}(q_i) = D_k\}$ is the
partition induced by construction.
The two coincide absent cross-domain migration.

The primary objective is a per-anchor, per-model BT score
$s_{i,D_k}$ reflecting comparative response quality on
$Q_{D_k}^{\text{adapted}}$; the validation objective is the
rank-agreement between $\hat{\boldsymbol{s}}_{D_k}$ and the
ground-truth per-domain accuracy vector $\mathbf{A}_{D_k}$.

\section{Directional Node Model}\label{sec:meth-vmf}

\subsection{von Mises--Fisher Density and MLE}

The vMF distribution on $\mathcal{S}^{d-1}$ with mean direction
$\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and concentration $\kappa \geq 0$ has density
\[
p(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}, \kappa)
= C_d(\kappa) \exp\!\left(\kappa \boldsymbol{\mu}^\top \mathbf{x}\right),
\qquad
C_d(\kappa) = \frac{\kappa^{d/2 - 1}}{(2\pi)^{d/2}\, I_{d/2 - 1}(\kappa)}.
\]
Given $N$ unit-vector observations, the MLE of $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ is
the normalised sample mean $\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}} =
\bar{\mathbf{x}} / \|\bar{\mathbf{x}}\|_2$.
The Banerjee closed-form approximation~\parencite{banerjee2005vmf}
\[
\hat{\kappa}_{\text{Banerjee}}
= \frac{\bar{R}\, d - \bar{R}^3}{1 - \bar{R}^2},
\qquad \bar{R} = \|\bar{\mathbf{x}}\|_2,
\]
avoids iterative root-finding for $\kappa$ and is accurate for
$d \geq 4$, $N \geq 5$.
The estimator is biased upward in high dimensions at small sample
sizes ($O(d/N)$ bias; approximately 27\% overestimation at depth-3
leaves with $N \approx 20$, $d = 1024$).
The mandatory Hornik--Grün correction~\parencite{hornik2014vmf}
\[
\hat{\kappa}_{\text{corrected}}
= \hat{\kappa}_{\text{Banerjee}} \cdot \frac{N - 1}{N(d - 2)}
\]
is applied at every estimation call site.
Numerically stable evaluation of $\log I_\nu(\kappa)$ at $d \geq 256$
via the Debye asymptotic expansion is given in
Appendix~\ref{app:numerics}.

\subsection{NiW Auxiliary Regulariser}

A Normal-inverse-Wishart (NiW) posterior is fit to each node's member
embeddings to provide a tractable Bayesian membership-scoring model
for routing and cross-link insertion.
The NiW is \textit{not} the conjugate prior for the vMF---the vMF's
conjugate is the vMF itself~\parencite{gopal2014mises}---but is used
as an engineering regulariser whose posterior predictive is a
multivariate Student-$t$, accurate when $\kappa$ is moderate to large
so that the tangent-plane approximation to $\mathcal{S}^{d-1}$ holds.
At near-uniform nodes ($\kappa < 1$) the approximation degrades; such
nodes are flagged as geometric leaves and excluded from splitting.

The prior mean is the parent's vMF centroid, the prior pseudocount is
$\kappa_0 = 1.0$, the prior degrees of freedom are
$\nu_0 = d_\ell + 2$, and the prior scale is
$\boldsymbol{\Lambda}_0 = \lambda_0 \mathbf{I}_{d_\ell}$ with
$\lambda_0 = 1/(\hat{\kappa}_{\text{parent}} d_\ell)$.
At $d_\ell \geq 512$ a diagonal approximation to
$\boldsymbol{\Lambda}_N$ is stored, reducing memory from $O(d^2)$ to
$O(d)$ per node.
The posterior update equations and the resulting Student-$t$
predictive density are given in Appendix~\ref{app:numerics}; the
predictive log-density is the scoring function used throughout
routing, migration, and cross-link decisions.

\section{Matryoshka Embedding Schedule}\label{sec:meth-mrl}

MRL~\parencite{kusupati2022mrl} trains a single embedding model such
that every prefix slice $\mathbf{x}_{1:d}$ is itself a meaningful
embedding, allowing the full vector to be stored once and sliced at
query time.
TaxoArena's depth-dimension schedule is
\[
d(\ell) =
\begin{cases}
128 & \ell = 0, \\
256 & \ell = 1, \\
512 & \ell = 2, \\
1024 & \ell \geq 3,
\end{cases}
\]
reflecting the coarse-to-fine information structure of MRL
embeddings: domain-level distinctions are separable in 128
dimensions, while sub-topic distinctions require the full
1024-dimensional prefix.
Re-normalisation after slicing is mandatory, since MRL guarantees
semantic meaningfulness of $\mathbf{x}_{1:d}$ but not unit norm:
\(
\mathbf{x}^{(\ell)}
= \mathbf{x}_{1:d_\ell} / \|\mathbf{x}_{1:d_\ell}\|_2.
\)

\section{Seed Initialisation and Cross-Domain Migration}\label{sec:meth-migration}

At iteration 0, each anchor $D_k$ is initialised with the canonical
partition and a vMF/NiW self-model fit at $d(0) = 128$; the anchor's
identity, depth, and \texttt{isAnchor} flag are set once and never
modified.

For each query $q_i$, the domain score under anchor $D_k$ is the
NiW log-posterior predictive
$z_{D_k}(\mathbf{x}_i) = \log p_{\text{NiW}}(\mathbf{x}_i^{(0)} \mid D_k)$.
Letting $D_{\text{seed}}(i)$ be $q_i$'s MMLU-Pro label and
$D^*(i) = \arg\max_k z_{D_k}(\mathbf{x}_i)$, query $q_i$ migrates from
$D_{\text{seed}}(i)$ to $D^*(i)$ iff
\[
z_{D^*(i)}(\mathbf{x}_i) - z_{D_{\text{seed}}(i)}(\mathbf{x}_i)
> \delta_{\text{mig}}.
\]
All $N$ migration decisions are evaluated in a single simultaneous
scoring pass before any reassignment, preventing cascading migrations
in which an early reassignment perturbs the NiW posteriors used to
score later queries.
Migration is applied only in the first construction iteration; a hard
cap of one pass prevents oscillation and stabilises
$Q_{D_k}^{\text{adapted}}$ before sub-domain splitting begins.
Anchor nodes are exempt from all destructive topology operations
regardless of migration volume, enforced by a
\texttt{TopologyViolationException} at the data-model level.

\section{Adaptive Splitting Oracle}\label{sec:meth-split}

Before applying vMF-EM refinement, a warm-start bisection is computed
via furthest-point $k$-means$++$ initialisation: the first centroid is
the node's vMF centroid, and the second is the member farthest by
angular distance, avoiding the dual-collapse failure mode of random
initialisation in high dimensions.

The Dasgupta cost of a hierarchical clustering
$T$ with cosine similarity $w_{ij} = \mathbf{x}_i^\top \mathbf{x}_j$
is $\text{cost}(T) = \sum_{i \neq j} w_{ij} |\text{leaves}(\text{LCA}_T(i,j))|$
\parencite{dasgupta2016cost}.
For a proposed bisection into $S_L, S_R$ with sizes $N_L, N_R$, the
normalised cost delta reduces to
\[
\delta_{\text{Dasgupta}}
= \frac{N_R W_L + N_L W_R}{N \cdot W_{\text{total}}},
\]
using the efficient identity
$W_L = \tfrac{1}{2}(\|\sum_{i \in S_L} \mathbf{x}_i\|_2^2 - N_L)$,
which reduces cost computation to $O(N_L d)$.
A split is accepted iff $\delta_{\text{Dasgupta}} > 0.02$ and both
children satisfy $|S_L|, |S_R| \geq n_{\min} = 15$.
The threshold has no closed-form optimum and trades over-splitting
against under-splitting; the bisecting procedure always proposes
exactly two children, and $D_{\max} = 3$ hard-caps recursion depth.
The greedy oracle provides no worst-case global-cost guarantee.

\section{Trickle Routing and Polyhierarchy}\label{sec:meth-routing}

At construction time, node $v$ with children $\{c_1, \ldots, c_K\}$
routes query embedding $\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{Q}_{\text{con}}$ to
child $c_k$ with probability
\[
P_{\text{con}}(c_k \mid \mathbf{x}, v)
= \frac{\exp\!\left(\hat{\kappa}_{c_k} \hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_{c_k}^\top
\mathbf{x}^{(\ell+1)} / \tau\right) p_{\text{NiW}}(\mathbf{x}^{(\ell+1)} \mid c_k)}
{\sum_{j=1}^{K} \exp\!\left(\hat{\kappa}_{c_j} \hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_{c_j}^\top
\mathbf{x}^{(\ell+1)} / \tau\right) p_{\text{NiW}}(\mathbf{x}^{(\ell+1)} \mid c_j)},
\]
combining the vMF concentration term with the NiW posterior
predictive, computed in log-space with log-sum-exp for multi-path
aggregation.
At evaluation time, held-out queries
$\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{Q}_{\text{eval}}$ are routed using the NiW
posterior predictive alone, with no $\hat{\kappa}\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}^\top\mathbf{x}$
term:
\[
P_{\text{eval}}(c_k \mid \mathbf{x}, v)
= \frac{p_{\text{NiW}}(\mathbf{x}^{(\ell+1)} \mid c_k)}
{\sum_{j=1}^{K} p_{\text{NiW}}(\mathbf{x}^{(\ell+1)} \mid c_j)}.
\]
This asymmetry is deliberate: the vMF term encodes the frozen
snapshot's own directional geometry and is appropriate for the
queries that shaped that geometry, whereas the NiW posterior
predictive is a calibrated density that generalises to unseen queries
without re-fitting.
A depth-decaying confidence funnel retains a query at its current
node as a leaf if its top assignment probability under the
applicable routing distribution falls below the funnel threshold at
that depth.
The funnel is anchored at $\gamma_0 = 0.999$ at depth 0 and relaxes
with depth, subject to a configurable floor
$\text{tauFunnelFloor}$ (default $0.90$) that lower-bounds the
depth-3 routing temperature so that leaf-level assignment remains
appropriately permissive rather than over-confident.

Primary assignment---the single best-scoring child used for fitting
and for all downstream statistics under the primary-path
convention---remains exclusive at every depth: exactly one child is
designated primary per query per depth, regardless of how many
children clear the funnel.
Secondary assignment, which is the sole basis for the bridges
introduced in Section~\ref{sec:meth-topology}, is granted whenever a
query's assignment margin to a non-primary node clears the gate
\[
\text{marginNats} = \text{assignmentCosineGap} \cdot
\frac{\kappa_{\text{eff}}}{\tau_{\text{eff}}}
> \delta_{\text{assign}},
\]
where $\text{assignmentCosineGap}$ is the cosine gap between the
query's best- and second-best-scoring node, and
$\kappa_{\text{eff}}, \tau_{\text{eff}}$ are the fitted concentration
and routing temperature at that depth (e.g.\ a gap of $0.03$ at
$\kappa_{\text{eff}} = 100$, $\tau_{\text{eff}} = 2$ yields a margin
of 1.5 nats).
Secondary assignment never overrides or duplicates the primary
assignment; it only creates candidate bridge evidence.
MMLU-Pro labels are never used in either routing distribution; they
are inspected only afterward, for the canonical-versus-adapted
divergence metrics (Section~\ref{sec:meth-validation}).

\section{Topological Optimisation}\label{sec:meth-topology}

A sequence of anchor-exempt operations runs after each splitting
phase.
\textbf{Sibling merge} pools two nodes when their vMF
Jensen--Shannon divergence falls below $\epsilon_{\text{sep}}$.
\textbf{Starved-leaf pruning} removes a leaf with fewer than $0.2$
times the mean sibling size, reassigning its members to the
highest-likelihood sibling.
\textbf{Passthrough collapse} promotes the sole child of a
single-child, memberless internal node.
\textbf{Soft-membership refitting} (\texttt{refitMuPerIteration})
recomputes each node's vMF centroid from its full posterior-weighted
membership rather than hard assignment alone, subject to a
no-update band: once a node's dominant posterior mass exceeds a high
threshold---$0.999$ at shallow depth, relaxing to $0.9975$ as depth
increases---the centroid is frozen for that iteration, preventing
noisy refitting once a node's membership has effectively stabilised.
\textbf{Bridge insertion} replaces a single global cross-link
threshold with two gated sources, both operating on the
$\text{marginNats}$ quantity defined in
Section~\ref{sec:meth-routing}.

\textit{Source-A (residual bridges).}
Residual leaves that received no primary queries after routing are
retained as bridge targets: any query whose secondary NiW posterior
score at a residual node clears the $\text{marginNats}$ gate is
linked to that node via a secondary edge, giving the residual node
evaluable membership without altering the query's primary leaf.

\textit{Source-B (cross-domain bridges).}
For a query whose primary leaf is $L$, a candidate secondary edge to
another leaf $L'$ (possibly under a different anchor) is proposed
whenever $L'$'s NiW posterior score for the query clears the
$\text{marginNats}$ gate.
The candidate is accepted only if it passes four sequential gates:
deduplication against existing edges; a coherence gate rejecting
edges that would push $L'$'s internal coherence below $0.70$; a
distinctness gate requiring the primary and secondary leaf profiles
to remain at least $0.92$ cosine-distinct; and a combined cycle
check and cross-domain dominant-category gate that blocks bridges
which would make a leaf's secondary membership dominate its
primary-anchor identity.
When all gates pass, a directed edge is added from the lowest common
ancestor of $L$ and $L'$ to $L'$, giving $L'$ a second parent while
$L$ retains both its terminal (leaf) status and its status as the
query's primary leaf.

A transitive reduction sweep follows both bridge sources to maintain
the minimal DAG invariant.

\section{Bradley--Terry Estimation}\label{sec:meth-bt}

\subsection{Model and MM Solver}

The BT model assigns each model $m_i$ a latent skill $s_i$ with
$P(m_i \succ m_j) = \sigma(s_i - s_j)$~\parencite{bradley1952rank}.
A zero-sum constraint $\sum_i s_i = 0$ fixes the additive degree of
freedom; ties contribute a half-win to each model, so
$W_{ij} + W_{ji} = N_{ij}$ without an explicit draw parameter.
The Minorisation-Maximisation update~\parencite{hunter2004mm}
\[
s_i^{(t+1)} = \log\!\left(
\frac{W_i}{\sum_{j \neq i} N_{ij} / \sigma(s_i^{(t)} - s_j^{(t)})}
\right),
\qquad W_i = \sum_{j \neq i} W_{ij},
\]
is applied cyclically with the zero-sum constraint re-enforced after
each sweep, until $\max_i |s_i^{(t+1)} - s_i^{(t)}| < 10^{-6}$ or a
500-iteration cap is reached.

\subsection{Standard Errors and Identifiability}

Standard errors are $\hat{\sigma}_i =
\sqrt{[\mathcal{I}(\hat{\mathbf{s}})^{-1}]_{ii}}$ from the observed
Fisher information matrix (full form in
Appendix~\ref{app:numerics}), using the pseudoinverse to account for
the rank-one deficiency induced by the zero-sum constraint.
BT parameters are identified only up to an additive constant per
connected component of the comparison graph; the connectivity
bootstrap phase of the scheduler (Section~\ref{sec:meth-scheduling})
guarantees every pair receives at least one comparison before
utility-driven scheduling begins.

\section{Active Match Scheduling}\label{sec:meth-scheduling}

The composite utility of comparison triple $(i,j,L)$ combines a
structural exploration term and an uncertainty resolution term under
a maturity-decaying mixing parameter $\alpha$:
\[
U(i,j,L) = \left[\alpha\, U_{\text{struct}}(i,j,L)
+ (1-\alpha)\, U_{\text{resolve}}(i,j,L)\right]
\times \left(1 - 0.3\, \frac{n_{ij}^{(L)}}{B_{ij}}\right).
\]
$U_{\text{struct}}$ rewards closely-ranked pairs
($\exp(-|\text{rank}_i - \text{rank}_j|)$).
$U_{\text{resolve}}$ is the binary entropy of the current win
probability $p_{ij} = \sigma(\hat{s}_i - \hat{s}_j)$, divided by the
combined variance $\hat{\sigma}_i^2 + \hat{\sigma}_j^2$, and weighted
by a KDE valley weight favouring midpoints in low-density regions of
the score distribution (bandwidth and weight derivation in
Appendix~\ref{app:numerics}).
The entropy term is maximised when $p_{ij} \approx 0.5$, prioritising
pairs whose current ranking is genuinely ambiguous; the variance
division then downweights pairs whose standard errors are already
large, since a single additional comparison contributes little
information relative to the pair's existing noise floor, and
upweights pairs that are ambiguous \textit{despite} already having
tight, well-estimated scores---the case where one more comparison is
most likely to be decisive.
This is the internally consistent reading of the utility term: entropy
identifies ambiguity, and dividing by variance targets that ambiguity
toward pairs where additional evidence is most informative rather than
toward pairs that are simply noisy.
$\alpha$ decays from $1.0$ to approximately $0.20$ as leaf maturity---a
weighted combination of standard-error reduction and completed-pair
fraction---approaches 1.
Within a scheduled pair, queries are selected by descending cosine
similarity to the leaf's vMF centroid, prioritising representative
queries.

\section{Stopping Policy}\label{sec:meth-stopping}

A pair $(i,j)$ at leaf $L$ resolves when any of three conditions
holds: separation
$|\hat{s}_i - \hat{s}_j| \geq 3.0(\hat{\sigma}_i + \hat{\sigma}_j)$;
budget exhaustion $n_{ij}^{(L)} \geq B_{ij}$; or utility floor
$U(i,j,L) < U_{\min}$.
A leaf converges when all pairs have resolved and the top-two
separation guard
$|\hat{s}_1^{(L)} - \hat{s}_2^{(L)}| > 1.5(\hat{\sigma}_1^{(L)} +
\hat{\sigma}_2^{(L)})$ holds, preventing premature convergence when
leading models remain statistically indistinguishable.
The arena halts globally when the query-weighted converged fraction
$f_{\text{conv}} = \sum_{L \in \mathcal{C}} |Q_L| /
\sum_{L \in \mathcal{L}} |Q_L|$ reaches $f^*$ for $R_{\text{stable}}$
consecutive rounds, with a hard escape valve at $B_{\max}$ total judge
calls.

\section{Upward Aggregation to GT Domains}\label{sec:meth-aggregation}

Domain-level win and comparison counts pool leaf-level sufficient
statistics under the primary-path convention,
$W_{ij,D_k} = \sum_{L \in \mathcal{D}(D_k)} W_{ij}^{(L)}$ and
analogously for $N_{ij,D_k}$, and a fresh MM fit produces
$\hat{\mathbf{s}}_{D_k}$.
Leaf-level parameters are never averaged: BT identification is fixed
only up to a per-component additive constant, so averaging
differently-normalised leaf scales produces no interpretable result,
whereas pooling counts and refitting yields a single shared scale.
A fractional-weight sensitivity analysis, in which a leaf with $k$
primary parents contributes $(1/k)$ of its counts to each, is
recorded under a separate \texttt{aggregationConvention} field for
comparison in Chapter~\ref{ch:results}.

Two parallel tracks are maintained per anchor: \textit{Track A
(canonical)} groups verdicts by MMLU-Pro label, producing
$\rho_{\text{canonical}}$; \textit{Track B (adapted)} groups by the
adapted partition, producing $\rho_{\text{adapted}}$.
The migration effect is
$\Delta\rho = \rho_{\text{adapted}} - \rho_{\text{canonical}}$; a
positive value indicates that geometric re-partitioning improves
rank agreement with ground-truth accuracy.

\section{Validation Against MMLU-Pro}\label{sec:meth-validation}

Ground-truth per-domain accuracy
$A_{i,D_k} = |\{q \in Q_{D_k} : m_i(q) = y_q^{\text{MMLU}}\}| / |Q_{D_k}|$
is computed on both partitions.
The judge rubric is \textit{reference-informed}: construction-set
reference answers inform the domain-conditioned evaluation criteria
generated by \texttt{GuidelineGenerator} (Section~\ref{subsec:judge}),
so that the judge's notion of quality is shaped by what correct
responses look like in the domain.
Ground-truth accuracy itself, and the reference answers for
$\mathcal{Q}_{\text{eval}}$ specifically, are never inspected during
arena evaluation; $A_{i,D_k}$ is computed strictly after all verdicts
are recorded and is used only for external, post-hoc validation.
Four rank-agreement metrics are reported per domain: Spearman $\rho$;
Kendall $\tau$ (preferred when $M$ is small); pairwise-winner
accuracy; and top-$k$ Jaccard agreement for $k \in \{3,5\}$.
The budget-fidelity curve plots $\rho$ against cumulative judge calls
for the adaptive scheduler versus random and round-robin baselines,
quantifying the marginal value of adaptive scheduling and the budget
at which additional calls yield diminishing returns.

\section{Methodological Assumptions}\label{sec:meth-assumptions}

Six assumptions underlie the methodology, each paired with a
diagnostic or sensitivity check: directional geometry reflects
semantic compatibility (checked via spherical silhouette score);
single-component vMF adequacy per node (checked via the Dasgupta
split test); local BT transitivity within coherent domains (supported
by domain-conditioned judging); judge reliability under domain
conditioning (partially tested by calibration analysis, not
structurally guaranteed); MMLU-Pro domains as a useful reporting
vocabulary (quantified by $\Delta\rho$); and query reuse inducing
within-query dependence across comparisons, which the Fisher
information standard errors treat as independent and therefore
slightly underestimate.

\section{Chapter Summary}

This chapter formalised the vMF/NiW node model and its bias-corrected
MLE, the MRL embedding schedule, the migration rule with its
single-pass oscillation guard, the Dasgupta splitting oracle, the
log-space trickle-routing softmax with confidence funnel and
primary/secondary assignment distinction, the topological
optimisation pass with soft-membership refitting and two-source
bridge insertion, the half-win BT
estimator with its MM solver and Fisher-information standard errors,
the composite-utility active scheduler, the three-level stopping
policy, sufficient-statistic pooling for upward aggregation under the
primary-path and fractional-weight conventions, and the four
rank-agreement validation metrics against MMLU-Pro.
Numerical-stability procedures supporting these models---log-Bessel
evaluation, the NiW posterior update algebra, the KDE bandwidth
derivation, and the full Fisher information matrix---are given in
Appendix~\ref{app:numerics}.
Hyperparameter values are specified and justified in
Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental-design}.
