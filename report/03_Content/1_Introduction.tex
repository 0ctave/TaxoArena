\chapter{Introduction}\label{ch:introduction}

\section{Motivation}\label{sec:motivation}

\subsection{The Routing Problem and the Agentic Turn}\label{subsec:the-agentic-turn}

The field of Artificial Intelligence is undergoing a structural shift
from systems that respond to individual prompts towards systems that
pursue goals autonomously across extended sequences of
decisions~\parencite{wissuchek2025agentic}.
This transition, the \textit{agentic turn}, marks the evolution of
Large Language Models (LLMs) from passive text-generation interfaces
into active participants within larger software
pipelines, in which complex tasks are
decomposed into sub-tasks and dispatched to specialised tools, APIs, or
models~\parencite{yao2023react}.
The quality of this dispatch depends critically on one question:
\textit{which model should handle a given sub-task?}

Answering it requires a fine-grained, up-to-date capability map of the
available models.
Deployed agentic systems are now widespread in
enterprise~\parencite{pwc2025survey}, yet the evaluation infrastructure
needed to build such a map remains largely absent: current benchmark
leaderboards report a single aggregate score per model, which conflates
performance across semantically heterogeneous tasks and gives no signal
about domain-specific strengths or failure modes.
A routing decision based on a global score is, at best, a coarse
approximation to the question the orchestrating agent actually needs
answered.

This thesis does not deploy a production router.
It builds and validates the substrate such a router would consume:
per-domain capability profiles at sufficient resolution to distinguish
models that are equal on average but opposite at the margin.

\subsection{The Evaluation Gap}\label{subsec:the-evaluation-gap}

The rapid deployment of LLMs has outpaced the development of evaluation
frameworks capable of distinguishing their capabilities at the
resolution required for informed routing---the diagnosis this section
develops and the literature review substantiates.
Two structural limitations characterise current practice.

\subsubsection*{The Static Benchmark Problem}

Traditional benchmarks such as MMLU~\parencite{hendrycks2021measuring}
and its successor MMLU-Pro~\parencite{wang2024mmlupro} assess capability
via fixed, editorially curated question sets organised into predefined
subject categories.
This design is brittle along two axes.
First, as models improve, the hardest questions saturate:
top-performing models converge toward indistinguishable scores, and the
benchmark loses the ability to discriminate between closely performing
systems~\parencite{boubdir2024elo}.
Second, the category structure is fixed at construction time, so
emerging capability dimensions---interdisciplinary topics, novel
application domains, or sub-skills at the boundary between existing
categories---have no representation unless the corpus is manually
rebuilt, creating systematic blind spots.

\subsubsection*{The Scalar Fallacy and Aggregation Bias}

Even when evaluation signals are available, current ranking systems
reduce multidimensional capability profiles to a single scalar score,
such as an Elo rating or a weighted
accuracy~\parencite{boubdir2024elo}.
This \textit{Scalar Fallacy} produces \textit{Aggregation Bias}:
averaging performance across all domains masks catastrophic failures in
specific subgroups and obscures profiles that differ only in
composition, not in mean~\parencite{raju2024constructing}.
Two models with identical global scores may differ sharply in their
domain profiles---one strong in formal reasoning and weak in applied
sciences, the other the reverse---yet a global leaderboard assigns them
identical routing priority for every task, when a routing policy should
assign them to entirely different sub-tasks.

\subsubsection*{A Routing Micro-Example}\label{subsec:routing-example}

To make the cost concrete, consider two models whose MMLU-Pro accuracy
is identical at the aggregate level---$0.72$ overall---but whose
per-domain profiles are mirror images.
Model~$A$ excels in formal domains: $0.91$ on Mathematics and $0.88$ on
Physics, but only $0.55$ on History and $0.51$ on Philosophy.
Model~$B$ is the reverse: $0.54$ on Mathematics and $0.50$ on Physics,
but $0.92$ on History and $0.90$ on Philosophy.
On any scalar leaderboard $A$ and $B$ are indistinguishable, and a router
that trusts that single number dispatches both an integration problem
and a historical-interpretation question to whichever model is
nominally first.
Yet the correct routing decision for a question requiring formal logical
inference is $A$, and for textual-source interpretation is $B$, each
with high confidence.
The aggregate score throws away exactly the information the routing
decision needs: the two models are \textit{equal on average and opposite
at the margin}.
A benchmark adequate for routing must therefore report capability at
the resolution of individual semantic sub-domains and distinguish
profiles that coincide in mean but differ in composition---the
resolution requirement the framework developed in this thesis is
designed to meet.

\subsection{The Limits of Conventional Judging}\label{subsec:judging-limits}

Measuring performance on knowledge-intensive tasks requires a judge.
Expert human annotation remains the gold standard but is economically
prohibitive at the scale required for continuous, multi-model,
multi-domain evaluation~\parencite{zheng2023judging}.
Automated scalar scoring introduces systematic calibration errors: the
judge has no stable internal reference for what a given score means
across domains, question types, or model generations.

Pairwise comparison resolves this by reducing judgement to a local,
contextualised question: is answer~A better than answer~B for this
specific query~\parencite{zheng2023judging}?
Yet LLM-as-a-judge systems exhibit biases even in pairwise mode:
\textit{positional bias} systematically favours the answer presented
first~\parencite{zheng2023judging}, and \textit{verbosity bias} favours
longer answers regardless of quality~\parencite{chen2024verbosity}.
Beyond these, judges suffer from \emph{guideline incompleteness}: any
finite rubric under-specifies the evaluation space, leaving cases where
the judge falls back on undocumented heuristics.
Evaluating within semantically homogeneous groups of questions reduces
the verbosity--quality confound and enables the tighter, domain-specific
rubrics a general-purpose judge cannot provide.

\section{Problem Statement}\label{sec:problem-statement}

The preceding discussion identifies four concrete, interconnected
problems that remain unresolved in current evaluation practice, each a
necessary precondition for the next; the two headline contributions of
this thesis address them in pairs (Section~\ref{sec:research-goal}).

\begin{enumerate}

    \item \textbf{Fixed-corpus brittleness.}
    Existing benchmarks use editorially defined categories that do not
    adapt to the evolving capability space of LLMs.
    A framework that uses editorial labels as a scaffold but refines
    domain structure geometrically can discover capability dimensions
    that matter without being permanently constrained by what the labels
    presuppose.

    \item \textbf{Scalar ranking and loss of capability resolution.}
    Aggregating performance into a single score conceals the domain
    structure of model capabilities, but routing requires per-domain
    rankings, not global leaderboards.
    A framework producing capability profiles---vectors of scores indexed
    by semantic dimension---provides the resolution scalar rankings
    discard.

    \item \textbf{Incoherent domain boundaries.}
    Induced evaluation structure is well-defined only if the resulting
    domains are semantically precise and internally homogeneous.
    A hierarchical decomposition of query space, derived from embedding
    geometry and formalised as a directed acyclic graph (DAG) of semantic
    sub-domains, provides principled boundaries whose extent is defined
    by the data rather than by editorial judgement.

    \item \textbf{Judging at scale with controllable bias.}
    Automated evaluation must trade off between calibration, bias, and
    domain sensitivity.
    A framework combining pairwise comparison with bidirectional order
    evaluation, domain-localised judging, and principled statistical
    aggregation can control the known biases while remaining tractable---but
    only if evaluation units are sufficiently homogeneous that outcomes
    are exchangeable within each domain.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Approach and Thesis Statement}\label{sec:approach}

TaxoArena works in three stages.
First, the 14 MMLU-Pro domain labels seed top-level anchor nodes, which
are then refined by embedding geometry: queries migrate to geometrically
closer anchors, and each anchor is recursively split into a DAG of
semantically coherent leaves.
Second, each leaf induces a reference-informed judge rubric from
construction-set queries and their correct answers.
Third, held-out model responses are evaluated pairwise within leaves and
aggregated via a Bradley--Terry model~\parencite{bradley1952rank,
hunter2004mm} into per-domain capability profiles.

This thesis designs, implements, and evaluates TaxoArena and tests a
single, two-part claim.
First, that answer-key-blind, domain-conditioned pairwise LLM
judges---evaluating models on held-out queries routed through a
geometrically induced semantic DAG---can recover the per-domain
capability rankings that MMLU-Pro produces using ground-truth accuracy on
selected high-support domain nodes.
Second, that geometry-adapted domain groupings improve the fidelity of
those rankings relative to the original editorial labels.

\textbf{Position of the DAG.}
The induced DAG is \textit{not} the contribution of this thesis; it is
the instrument by which the per-domain ranking resolution required for
routing is made available.
The scientific content is the demonstration that this instrument---a
geometrically induced, label-seeded semantic DAG coupled with
reference-informed, answer-key-blind judging---is sufficient to recover
ground-truth per-domain rankings, and that geometric adaptation of the
domain partition adds fidelity over editorial labels.
The DAG construction is therefore presented at the level needed to
justify that the judge domains it produces are granular and internally
coherent; its statistical derivations are deferred to
Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology} and Appendix~\ref{app:numerics}.

Two properties of the evaluation design are central to its validity.
The \textit{judge-generation phase} uses correct MMLU-Pro answers from
the construction query set to induce domain-specific rubrics; the system
is \textit{reference-informed} at construction time.
The \textit{arena evaluation phase} evaluates held-out responses without
access to those queries' correct answers; the evaluation is
\textit{answer-key-blind} at test time, and the MMLU-Pro accuracy oracle
is revealed only after all matches are complete.
The title term \textit{Dynamic Domains} refers to this dual property:
domain boundaries are seeded from MMLU-Pro labels but subsequently
adapted by geometric evidence---queries migrate to a geometrically more
appropriate domain, and new sub-domain leaves emerge from recursive
splitting---so the evaluation dimensions reflect the structure of the
data rather than a fixed editorial taxonomy.

\section{Contributions}\label{sec:research-goal}

\begin{enumerate}

    \item \textbf{Seed-guided geometric taxonomy construction.}
    TaxoArena induces a semantic DAG over the full 14-domain MMLU-Pro
    corpus by seeding 14 anchor nodes from ground-truth labels and then
    letting embedding geometry refine them.
    Queries migrate to geometrically closer anchors, producing an
    \textit{adapted partition} that may diverge from the original
    \textit{canonical} label partition; each anchor is recursively split
    under a chance-corrected separation score---accepting a proposed
    partition only when its within-cluster scatter is sufficiently below
    that expected under a uniformly random same-size partition---into
    internally coherent leaves, with polyhierarchical DAG edges added by
    gated topology operations rather than by query multi-assignment
    during fitting.
    During construction each query holds weighted membership in every
    sibling whose posterior responsibility clears a fixed membership
    floor; the distribution fits use these normalised soft weights, while
    a \textit{primary} (argmax) assignment is retained as a bookkeeping
    convention for aggregation.
    This construction is the instrument that produces granular, internally
    homogeneous judge domains; its statistical choices---a fixed
    256-dimensional Matryoshka Representation Learning (MRL)
    slice~\parencite{kusupati2022mrl}, a small-sample-corrected vMF
    concentration estimator, and a unified separation threshold governing
    splitting, merging, sibling distinctness, and residual-split
    viability---are defended as a coherent set in
    Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology}.

    \item \textbf{Reference-informed, answer-key-blind arena evaluation.}
    For each leaf, TaxoArena generates a domain-specific judge by
    presenting construction queries---with their correct answers---to an
    LLM in a MapReduce induction pipeline, producing a rubric that
    encodes what correct reasoning looks like in that sub-domain without
    referencing specific answers.
    Within each selected node the arena evaluates held-out model responses
    pairwise using this rubric: each comparison is run in both answer
    orders with analysis-before-verdict prompting, and disagreements
    between the orderings are treated as ties.
    Arena routing, like construction routing, permits a query to enter
    multiple sufficiently compatible leaves (capped by a per-query
    assignment limit); when a held-out query enters $k$ leaves, its
    verdicts contribute with weight $1/k$ to each leaf's win-count
    matrix, preventing the same comparison from being double-counted.
    This leaf-level fractional weighting is distinct from leaf-to-ancestor
    aggregation, which pools sufficient statistics under a primary-path
    convention (Chapter~\ref{ch:system-architecture}).
    Results are aggregated via Bradley--Terry, producing coherent
    per-leaf, per-domain, and corpus-wide capability profiles.

\end{enumerate}

An active matchmaking scheduler that prioritises model pairs with high
rating uncertainty and declares per-leaf convergence from
Bradley--Terry standard errors keeps judge-call cost tractable; it is an
engineering component supporting Contribution~2 rather than an
independent contribution
(Section~\ref{sec:meth-scheduling} in Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology}).

\section{Research Questions}\label{sec:research-questions}

\begin{enumerate}

    \item[\textbf{RQ1.}] Do answer-key-blind arena rankings agree with
    ground-truth MMLU-Pro accuracy on held-out queries, on the selected
    domain nodes?
    \textit{Primary metrics: Kendall} $\tau$\textit{, pairwise-winner
    accuracy, top-}$k$\textit{ Jaccard; Spearman} $\rho$
    \textit{secondary, with bootstrap confidence intervals given the
    small model count.}

    \item[\textbf{RQ2.}] Does evaluation within geometry-adapted leaf
    groupings improve ranking fidelity over flat MMLU-Pro domain
    groupings?
    \textit{Primary metric:} $\Delta\tau = \tau_{\text{adapted}} -
    \tau_{\text{canonical}}$ (Kendall-based), reported per selected
    node; $\Delta\rho$ (Spearman-based) secondary.

\end{enumerate}

Two further analyses are reported as non-headline diagnostics rather
than claims the thesis depends on: a construction-validity check that
the induced DAG is geometrically coherent relative to unsupervised
baselines (Dendrogram Purity, Weighted Leaf Purity, Spherical
Silhouette, Total Dasgupta Cost, Sackin index, Routing ECE), and an
optional test of whether the adaptive
scheduler recovers the same ranking at lower judge-call cost than random
scheduling.

\section{Scope and Validity}\label{sec:intro-scope}

This thesis evaluates the mechanism at high statistical power on
selected high-support domain nodes rather than spreading the same
judge-call budget thinly across all 14 domains: the arena roster is 12
models on one or two ex-ante-selected nodes (Math), with the selection
rule specified in Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental-design}.
The reported arena results are therefore a selected-domain
proof-of-concept, not a global validation across all MMLU-Pro domains;
generalisation at matched power is identified as future work
(Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion}).

Two properties secure the evaluation's validity.
Construction and rubric induction use construction-set queries and their
correct answers; the arena evaluates held-out responses without access
to those queries' correct answers; and the MMLU-Pro accuracy oracle is
revealed only after all matches are complete, so no arena-test signal
can feed back into any system parameter.
The three-way construction/tuning-validation/arena-test partition, the
information-separation table, and the leakage analysis that enforces
this boundary are specified in Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental-design}.

\section{Thesis Structure}\label{sec:thesis-structure}

The remainder of this thesis is organised as follows.

Chapter~\ref{ch:literature-review} surveys five bodies of prior work:
LLM evaluation benchmarks, hierarchical taxonomy construction,
LLM-as-a-judge reliability, pairwise ranking models, and query routing,
with each section concluding with the design choice it motivates.
Chapter~\ref{ch:system-architecture} describes the system design as
two coupled subsystems: a taxonomy construction pipeline (seed
initialisation, iterative splitting and topology optimisation, and
construction completion) and an arena evaluation subsystem (routing,
per-leaf MapReduce judge-rubric induction, match scheduling,
Bradley--Terry aggregation, and upward propagation).
Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology} formalises the arena's statistical
machinery---the Bradley--Terry likelihood and MM algorithm, the active
scheduling utility, and the sufficient-statistic upward aggregation
procedure---and summarises the taxonomy-construction formalism (the
vMF node model with small-sample-corrected MLE, the fixed-dimension
MRL slice, the chance-corrected separation score, and the
membership-floor trickle routing), whose full treatment is deferred to
Appendix~\ref{app:dag-formalism}; numerical-stability derivations are
deferred to Appendix~\ref{app:numerics}.
Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental-design} specifies the experimental
configuration: corpus and domain selection, the three-way split protocol
with pre-committed random seed, the L9 hyperparameter tuning protocol,
the ex-ante domain-node selection rule, DAG and arena hyperparameters, the
evaluated model set, judge configuration, baselines, and pre-specified
metrics and thresholds.
Chapter~\ref{ch:results} presents empirical results, covering DAG
construction quality, the trickle-test routing diagnostic, arena ranking
validity~(RQ1), and adapted-vs-canonical fidelity~(RQ2).
Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion} interprets the findings, addresses the two
research questions directly, and discusses construct validity and
limitations---including the selected-domain, proof-of-concept scope of
the arena evaluation.
Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion} summarises the contributions and outlines
future work, including production router deployment, multi-corpus
transfer, and online domain discovery.
