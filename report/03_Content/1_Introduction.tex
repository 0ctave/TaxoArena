
\chapter{Introduction}\label{ch:introduction}

\section{Motivation}\label{sec:motivation}

\subsection{The Agentic Turn}\label{subsec:the-agentic-turn}

The field of Artificial Intelligence is undergoing a structural shift from systems that respond to individual prompts towards systems that pursue goals autonomously across extended sequences of decisions~\parencite{wissuchek2025agentic}.
This transition, referred to in recent literature as the \textit{agentic turn}, marks the evolution of Large Language Models (LLMs) from passive text-generation interfaces into active participants within larger software pipelines~\parencite{haidemariam2025turn}.
In agentic architectures, complex tasks are decomposed into sub-tasks that are dispatched to specialised tools, APIs, or models~\parencite{schick2023toolformer, yao2023react}.
The quality of this decomposition and dispatch depends critically on one question: \textit{which model should handle a given sub-task?}

Answering this question requires a fine-grained, up-to-date capability map of the available models.
As of 2025, approximately \qty{79}{\percent} of enterprises have begun deploying agentic systems~\parencite{pwc2025survey}, yet the evaluation infrastructure needed to build such a capability map remains largely absent.
Current benchmark leaderboards report a single aggregate score per model, which conflates performance across semantically heterogeneous tasks and provides no signal about domain-specific strengths or failure modes.
A routing decision based on a global Elo score is, at best, a coarse approximation to the question the orchestrating agent actually needs answered.

\subsection{The Evaluation Gap}\label{subsec:the-evaluation-gap}

The rapid deployment of LLMs and agentic systems has outpaced the development of evaluation frameworks capable of distinguishing their capabilities at the resolution required for informed routing~\parencite{yan2025evaluation, shukla2025evaluating}.
Two structural limitations characterise current practice.

\subsubsection*{The Static Benchmark Problem}

Traditional benchmarks such as MMLU~\parencite{hendrycks2020measuring} and its successor MMLU-Pro~\parencite{wang2024mmlu} assess capability via fixed, editorially curated sets of questions organised into predefined subject categories.
This design is inherently brittle along two axes.
First, as models improve, the hardest questions saturate: top-performing models converge toward indistinguishable scores, and the benchmark loses its ability to discriminate between closely performing systems~\parencite{simmering2025benchmarks}.
Second, the category structure is fixed by human editors at construction time.
Emerging capability dimensions --- interdisciplinary topics, novel application domains, or sub-skills that lie at the boundary between existing categories --- have no representation in the benchmark unless the corpus is manually rebuilt.
This creates systematic blind spots: a model may be systematically strong or weak at, for example, the intersection of formal logic and legal reasoning, yet no existing category captures this sub-domain, and no score reflects it.

The underlying problem is that a fixed corpus of questions is a static approximation of a dynamic capability space.
As the task landscape evolves, the benchmark must be continuously extended to remain informative.
A benchmark framework designed to be extensible --- one that can absorb new queries, discover new evaluation dimensions from the data geometry, and update rankings incrementally --- directly addresses this brittleness.

\subsubsection*{The Scalar Fallacy and Aggregation Bias}

Even when evaluation signals are available, current ranking systems reduce multidimensional capability profiles to a single scalar score, such as an Elo rating or a weighted accuracy~\parencite{boubdir2024elo}.
This \textit{Scalar Fallacy} produces \textit{Aggregation Bias}: averaging performance across all domains masks catastrophic failures in specific subgroups and obscures capability profiles that differ only in composition, not in mean~\parencite{shukla2025evaluating}.

Two models with identical global scores may differ sharply in their domain profiles: one strong in formal reasoning and weak in applied sciences, the other the reverse.
A global leaderboard assigns them identical routing priority for every task, when a routing policy should assign them to entirely different sub-tasks.
Addressing this requires evaluation dimensions that are derived from the semantic structure of the task space itself, rather than imposed by editorial convention --- a benchmark that organises its ranking space around the capabilities it actually needs to measure.

\subsection{The Limits of Conventional Judging}\label{subsec:judging-limits}

Measuring performance on knowledge-intensive tasks requires a judge.
Expert human annotation is the gold standard but is economically prohibitive at the scale required for continuous, multi-model, multi-domain evaluation~\parencite{li2026agencybench}.
Automated scalar scoring --- asking a model to assign a numerical quality score --- introduces systematic calibration errors: the judge has no stable internal reference point for what a score of seven means across different domains, question types, or model generations.

Pairwise comparison resolves the calibration problem by reducing judgement to a local, contextualised question: is answer~A better than answer~B for this specific question~\parencite{zheng2024judging}?
However, LLM-as-a-judge systems exhibit two residual biases even in pairwise mode.
\textit{Positional bias} causes judges to systematically favour the answer presented first~\parencite{zheng2024judging}.
\textit{Verbosity bias} causes judges to favour longer answers regardless of their actual quality~\parencite{li2024verbosity}.
These biases are domain-sensitive: a verbose answer in a formal proof task may genuinely be more complete, while a verbose answer in a factual recall task is merely long.
Evaluating within semantically homogeneous groups of questions reduces the confound between verbosity and quality that a domain-agnostic judge cannot resolve.

\section{Problem Statement}\label{sec:problem-statement}

The preceding discussion identifies three concrete, interconnected problems that remain unresolved in current evaluation practice.

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{Fixed-corpus brittleness.}
Existing benchmarks are organised around editorially defined subject categories that do not adapt to the evolving capability space of LLMs.
Blind spots at category boundaries, saturation of hard questions, and the absence of cross-domain capability dimensions are structural consequences of fixing the query corpus and its organisation at construction time.
A framework that induces its evaluation structure from query embeddings, rather than from human labels, can discover the dimensions that matter without presupposing what they are.

\item \textbf{Scalar ranking and loss of capability resolution.}
Aggregating performance into a single score conceals the domain structure of model capabilities.
The routing problem in agentic systems requires per-domain rankings, not global leaderboards.
A framework that produces a capability profile --- a vector of scores indexed by semantic dimension --- provides the resolution that scalar rankings discard.

\item \textbf{Judging at scale with controllable bias.}
Automated evaluation must be scalable, yet current approaches trade off between calibration (pairwise is well-calibrated but expensive), bias (scalar scoring is cheap but poorly calibrated), and domain sensitivity (general-purpose judges ignore domain-specific quality criteria).
A framework that combines pairwise comparison with position randomisation, domain-localised judging, and principled statistical aggregation can control the known biases while remaining computationally tractable.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Research Goal}\label{sec:research-goal}

This thesis designs, implements, and evaluates \textbf{TaxoArena}: a query-adaptive evaluation framework that constructs a domain taxonomy from unlabelled query embeddings and produces per-domain model rankings, with the explicit goal of enabling routing-aware model selection in knowledge-intensive agentic pipelines.

The framework is structured around three integrated contributions.

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{Geometry-driven taxonomy construction.}
Rather than organising evaluation queries by predefined subject labels, TaxoArena induces a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) taxonomy from the geometric structure of query embeddings in a Matryoshka Representation Learning (MRL) space~\parencite{kusupati2022mrl}.
Each node in the DAG is modelled as a von Mises--Fisher (vMF) distribution on the unit hypersphere; recursive splitting is governed by a Dasgupta cost oracle~\parencite{dasgupta2016cost}.
The DAG structure, rather than a strict tree, is motivated by the continuity of the embedding space: queries near the geometric boundary between two nodes are assigned to both via soft routing, reflecting genuine semantic overlap rather than a forced categorical choice.
This design makes the taxonomy extensible: new queries can be routed into existing nodes via Bayesian NiW posterior scoring or, when a sufficient accumulation of geometrically novel queries is detected, trigger a principled split that creates a new evaluation dimension without invalidating existing rankings.

\item \textbf{Multidimensional arena evaluation.}
For each leaf node of the taxonomy, TaxoArena runs a pairwise Bradley--Terry arena using an LLM judge~\parencite{bradley1952rank}.
Position randomisation and domain-localised judging address the positional and verbosity biases identified in~\autoref{subsec:judging-limits}.
The result is a per-leaf capability score for each evaluated model --- a multidimensional profile that directly answers the routing question: given a query assigned to leaf~$L$, which model ranks highest at~$L$?
This is the \textit{query-adaptive multidimensional ranking} of the thesis title.

\item \textbf{Budget-efficient convergence via active scheduling.}
A round-robin evaluation of all model pairs at all leaf nodes scales as $O(M^2 \cdot N_L)$ in judge calls, where $M$ is the number of models and $N_L$ is the total query pool across leaves.
TaxoArena replaces round-robin with an active matchmaking scheduler that prioritises pairs with high rating uncertainty, and a stopping policy that declares convergence per leaf based on Bradley--Terry standard errors rather than on a fixed call budget.
The hypothesis is that stable per-leaf rankings are achievable with substantially fewer judge calls than round-robin, validated by Spearman rank correlation stability.

\end{enumerate}

The scope of this thesis is the design and first experimental validation of TaxoArena on the MMLU-Pro mathematics sub-corpus~\parencite{wang2024mmlu}.
The extensibility property --- incremental ingestion of new queries and discovery of new leaf nodes --- is explored as a design criterion and analysed theoretically; a full longitudinal evaluation across multiple corpus updates is identified as future work.

\section{Thesis Structure}\label{sec:thesis-structure}

The remainder of this thesis is organised as follows.
Chapter~\ref{ch:literature} surveys the relevant literature on hierarchical text clustering, LLM evaluation frameworks, pairwise ranking models, and Matryoshka embeddings.
Chapter~\ref{ch:system-architecture} describes the system architecture of TaxoArena, covering the six-phase taxonomy construction pipeline, the arena subsystem, and the infrastructure.
Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology} formalises the statistical and geometric methods underlying each component.
Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental-setup} specifies the experimental configuration, ablation matrix, and evaluation metrics.
Chapter~\ref{ch:results} presents empirical results across taxonomy quality and arena evaluation metrics.
Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion} discusses the findings, limitations, and implications for the routing use case.
Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion} summarises contributions and outlines directions for future work.
