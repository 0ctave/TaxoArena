
\chapter{System Architecture}\label{ch:system-architecture}

TaxoArena comprises two coupled subsystems: a taxonomy construction
pipeline that induces a domain sub-structure from the geometric structure
of query embeddings within a set of fixed seed domains, and an arena
evaluation subsystem that produces per-leaf and per-domain
Bradley--Terry rankings through adaptive pairwise comparison and
upward score aggregation.
The chapter describes each subsystem at the architectural level---component
responsibilities, data flows, state representations, and interface
contracts---deferring formal derivations and algorithmic proofs to
Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology}.

A central structural decision distinguishes this design from a purely
unsupervised taxonomy induction approach: the 14 domain labels of
MMLU-Pro initialise 14 immutable anchor nodes at depth~0---fixed
organisational boundaries within which all geometric refinement
proceeds (Section~\ref{sec:arch-taxonomy}).
This design preserves a direct correspondence between the induced
taxonomy and MMLU-Pro's domain taxonomy, enabling the per-anchor
Bradley--Terry rankings produced by upward score aggregation to be
compared directly against MMLU-Pro accuracy computed on the held-out
query subset.

\section{Design Goals and Scope}\label{sec:arch-goals}

TaxoArena is designed around three primary goals, each of which imposes
constraints on the architectural choices described in the remainder of
this chapter.

\textbf{Goal 1: Seed-guided geometric refinement.}
The 14 domain labels of MMLU-Pro are used to initialise 14 immutable
anchor nodes at depth~0.
Within each anchor, the sub-structure is induced exclusively from the
geometric arrangement of query embeddings: no editorial label, ontology,
or human annotation governs the splitting, merging, or cross-linking
of internal and leaf nodes below the anchor level.
Query membership across anchors is mutable: a query initially assigned
to its MMLU-Pro label may migrate to a geometrically more appropriate
anchor during iterative refinement (Section~\ref{subsec:migration}).

\textbf{Goal 2: Budget efficiency.}
Arena evaluations are conducted with as few judge calls as the
statistical task permits.
The comparison budget is allocated adaptively, concentrating calls on
the model pairs that are hardest to distinguish at each leaf, and
evaluation terminates per leaf when a formal convergence criterion is
satisfied rather than after a fixed number of rounds.
At the global level, convergence is declared by a query-weighted
stability condition rather than a simple leaf-count fraction, preventing
small boundary leaves from prematurely terminating the arena.

\textbf{Goal 3: Reference-informed, domain-conditioned judging.}
Each pairwise comparison is evaluated by a judge whose prompt is
conditioned on domain-specific evaluation criteria generated at
taxonomy construction time.
The conditioning is derived from representative queries drawn from the
leaf's construction pool---queries whose MMLU-Pro reference answers are
available and are provided to \texttt{GuidelineGenerator} as evidence
of what a correct response looks like in this topic area.
The resulting rubric is \textit{reference-informed}: it encodes quality
criteria that reflect the correct-answer standard of the construction
corpus, while held-out evaluation queries and their answers do not enter
this process.
Dual-call consistency filtering and tie allowance are applied at the
prompt level to suppress the two most tractable systematic biases
identified in Section~\ref{sec:llm-as-a-judge}.

\subsection*{Scope Boundary}

TaxoArena is an \textit{offline profiling system}.
Its output is a set of per-leaf and per-anchor Bradley--Terry score
vectors, one per evaluated model, organised by the nodes of the induced
DAG.
It does not implement a query router, a model cascade, or an inference
dispatcher.
The use of these profiles as input to a downstream routing system is
outside the scope of this thesis and is identified as future work in
Section~\ref{sec:future-work}.

\subsection*{Requirements Traceability}

Each gap identified in Chapter~\ref{ch:literature-review} maps to a
dedicated architectural component: corpus brittleness to the induced
taxonomy pipeline with MMLU-Pro-aligned anchors, the missing
statistical node model and splitting criterion to
\texttt{TaxonomyFitter} and \texttt{TaxonomySplitter}, judge bias and
miscalibration to \texttt{JudgeService}'s dual-call filtering and
\texttt{GuidelineGenerator}'s reference-informed rubrics, comparison
cost and the missing convergence criterion to
\texttt{BtMatchScheduler} and \texttt{BtStoppingPolicy}, and the
domain-blindness of global BT rankings to per-leaf fitting with
upward aggregation (\S\ref{sec:arch-taxonomy},
\S\ref{sec:arch-arena}).

\section{System Overview and Data Flow}\label{sec:arch-overview}

TaxoArena is structured as three subsystems connected by four
persistent SQLite stores.
The subsystems execute sequentially: the Embedding subsystem
pre-computes all vector representations; the Taxonomy subsystem
constructs the domain DAG from those representations within the
fixed anchor structure; the Arena subsystem evaluates models within
the induced leaf nodes and aggregates scores upward to anchor level.
The databases serve as interface artefacts between subsystems,
decoupling their execution and enabling each phase to be re-run
independently without recomputing upstream results.

\subsection*{Data Flow}

The data flow follows seven stages.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Embedding.}
All corpus queries are embedded using Qwen3-Embedding and stored in
\texttt{embeddings\_cache.db}.
A single fixed prefix slice of dimension $d = 256$ is extracted and
$\ell_2$-normalised to the unit hypersphere; the same dimension is
used at every taxonomy depth (Section~\ref{subsec:dim-schedule}).

\item \textbf{Anchor initialisation.}
Each query is assigned to the anchor node corresponding to its
MMLU-Pro ground-truth domain label, creating 14 depth-0 partitions.
These assignments are the starting point for iterative refinement;
cross-domain migration may reassign queries during subsequent phases.

\item \textbf{Iterative refinement.}
The six-phase taxonomy loop runs within each anchor, inducing a
sub-domain DAG by recursive vMF-based splitting and topological
optimisation.
When the iteration budget completes, the final DAG state is
serialised as an immutable snapshot to \texttt{snapshots.db}.

\item \textbf{Held-out routing.}
A reserved subset of queries not used during taxonomy construction
is routed through the frozen snapshot by the same posterior-responsibility
trickle used at construction time: at each node, children are scored by
their own vMF log-density and admitted when their softmax responsibility
clears the \texttt{membershipFloor}.
Each routed query is assigned to one or more target leaves (capped at
\texttt{maxLeafAssignments}), forming the per-leaf query pool for arena
evaluation.

\item \textbf{Response generation and judging.}
For each query in a leaf's pool, responses are generated by each
evaluated model and cached.
The \texttt{BtMatchScheduler} selects comparison pairs; the
\texttt{JudgeService} dispatches dual-call pairwise comparisons
using a reference-informed, domain-conditioned prompt;
verdicts are persisted to \texttt{ratings.db}.

\item \textbf{Upward score aggregation.}
Verdict sufficient statistics ($W_{ij}$, $N_{ij}$) are propagated
from leaves to ancestor nodes by pooling under the primary-path
convention; a new Bradley--Terry model is fit at each ancestor.

\item \textbf{Validation interface.}
Per-anchor BT rankings are compared against MMLU-Pro accuracy
computed on the held-out query subset, partitioned by domain,
providing an external validity check on the arena's discriminative
power.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Taxonomy Construction Pipeline}\label{sec:arch-taxonomy}

Before iterative refinement begins, every corpus query is assigned to
one of 14 immutable anchor nodes corresponding to its MMLU-Pro
ground-truth domain label, creating 14 depth-0 partitions.
This ground-truth assignment is an initialisation only, and it is
deliberately preserved through the first construction iteration: the
first split/optimise/refit pass runs on the ground-truth bootstrap
(every query on its labelled anchor with weight $1.0$), and geometric
trickle reassignment begins at iteration~2, once real sub-domain
structure exists for routing to act on.
From iteration~2 onward, a query may end up under a geometrically more
appropriate anchor simply because re-routing places it there;
\textit{migration} is emergent from this re-routing, not a separate
gated mechanism, and the \textit{adapted} partition is defined as the
anchor assignment after construction completes, which can differ from
the \textit{canonical}, editorial one.
This canonical-versus-adapted distinction is the basis of the
geometry-correction research question (RQ2, Chapter~\ref{ch:results});
the routing mechanics are given in Appendix~\ref{app:dag-formalism}.

Within each anchor, \texttt{TaxonomyEngine} then executes a six-phase
loop for a fixed iteration budget: (1)~embedding-slice extraction and
re-normalisation, (2)~vMF/NiW model fitting per node, (3)~soft
trickle-routing of queries to children (skipped in iteration~1 as
described above), (4)~adaptive splitting of nodes whose
proposed partition clears the chance-corrected separation threshold,
(5)~topological optimisation (sibling merging, starved-leaf pruning,
removal of structurally redundant single-child nodes, and gated
cross-link/bridge insertion), and
(6)~a convergence check that measures the graph edit distance to the
preceding iteration's DAG and either triggers the next iteration,
stops early once the structure has been quiescent for five consecutive
iterations, or freezes the DAG when the budget is exhausted.
No editorial label, ontology, or human annotation governs splitting,
merging, or cross-linking below the anchor level: all sub-anchor
structure is induced purely from the geometric arrangement of query
embeddings.
When construction terminates---by early stopping or budget
exhaustion---\texttt{TaxonomyEngine}
serialises the resulting DAG to \texttt{snapshots.db} as an immutable
snapshot (Section~\ref{sec:arch-dag}); the Arena subsystem
(Section~\ref{sec:arch-arena}) reads exclusively from this frozen
structure, so no arena run can alter the taxonomy it evaluates
against.

The full formal treatment of this pipeline---the vMF/NiW node model,
the fixed-dimension embedding-slice rationale, the iteration-1
bootstrap and emergent migration, the chance-corrected splitting
score, the membership-floor trickle routing, and
the topological-optimisation operators---is given in
Appendix~\ref{app:dag-formalism}.

\section{Taxonomy DAG Data Model}\label{sec:arch-dag}

Every node in the taxonomy is represented as a \texttt{GraphNode}
object persisted to \texttt{snapshots.db}
(principal fields: \texttt{id}, \texttt{label},
\texttt{originalCategory}, \texttt{depth}, tree \texttt{children},
\texttt{crossLinkChildren}, \texttt{parents}, soft
\texttt{queryWeights}, the vMF self-model
$(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \kappa)$ with its log-normaliser, the diagonal NiW
posterior $(m_N, \kappa_N, \nu_N, \Lambda_N)$, and residual-query
bookkeeping); the fixed-embedding-dimension and
polyhierarchy/cross-link mechanics
that shape this data model are given in
Appendix~\ref{app:dag-formalism}.
The two properties below---node typing and snapshot
immutability---are kept here because the arena subsystem
(Section~\ref{sec:arch-arena}) depends on both directly: leaves are
the arena's query pool, and the frozen-snapshot property is what
makes an arena run reproducible against a fixed taxonomy.

\subsection*{Node Types}

Four node types are distinguished by their structural role.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Anchor} (\texttt{isAnchor = true}, \texttt{depth = 0}).
Created during seed initialisation from MMLU-Pro ground-truth labels;
immutable and present in every snapshot, as specified in
Section~\ref{sec:arch-taxonomy}.

\item \textbf{Internal.}
A non-anchor node with at least one child.
Created by the splitter when a bisection is accepted.
Subject to merge and collapse if the topological optimiser determines
the node is redundant.

\item \textbf{Leaf.}
A non-anchor node with no children after the final iteration.
Carries a vMF self-model and NiW posterior; its member set forms
the query pool for arena evaluation.

\item \textbf{Residual.}
A leaf that received no primary queries after routing.
Excluded from arena evaluation unless Source-A bridging
(Appendix~\ref{app:dag-formalism}) assigns it evaluable secondary
membership; otherwise retained in the snapshot for diagnostic
purposes only.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Snapshot Immutability}

When construction completes, \texttt{TaxonomyEngine}
serialises the current DAG to \texttt{snapshots.db} and sets an
\texttt{isFrozen} flag on the snapshot record.
A frozen snapshot is read-only: no subsequent taxonomy operation may
modify its nodes or edges.
The Arena subsystem reads exclusively from frozen snapshots,
identified by a \texttt{snapshotId} UUID recorded in \texttt{ratings.db}
alongside every verdict.
Multiple snapshots from successive construction runs are retained,
providing a version history that supports ablation studies over
taxonomy construction hyperparameters.

\section{Arena Architecture}\label{sec:arch-arena}

\subsection{Arena Lifecycle and Per-Leaf State}

The arena subsystem is initialised from a frozen snapshot identified
by a \texttt{snapshotId} and a specified set of evaluated models
$\mathcal{M} = \{m_1, \ldots, m_M\}$.
On startup, \texttt{BenchmarkService} routes the reserved held-out
query set through the frozen DAG using the same posterior-responsibility
trickle as construction: at each node, every child whose
temperature-1 softmax responsibility over the children's own vMF
log-densities clears the \texttt{membershipFloor} is descended into,
the same floor bounds the cumulative path probability, and the
resulting leaf set is capped at \texttt{maxLeafAssignments} per query.
The resulting per-leaf query pools $\{Q_L\}_{L \in \mathcal{L}}$ are
fixed for the duration of the arena run.
When a held-out query $q$ is assigned to $k > 1$ leaves, its verdicts
contribute with weight $1/k$ to each leaf's win-count matrix; this
prevents the same comparison from inflating the sufficient statistics
of multiple leaves and is distinct from the primary-path convention
used for DAG polyhierarchy during upward aggregation
(Section~\ref{subsec:aggregation}).

For each target leaf $L$, \texttt{BenchmarkService} instantiates a
\texttt{LeafArenaState} record containing: the BT log-strength vector
$\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(L)}$ and per-parameter standard errors
$\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{(L)}$, both initialised to $\mathbf{0}$ and
$\infty$ respectively; the win-count matrix
$W^{(L)} \in \mathbb{N}^{M \times M}$ and comparison-count matrix
$N^{(L)}$, initialised to zero; the per-pair state map; the leaf
convergence state; the pre-generated evaluation guidelines; and the
response cache.

The round loop then executes until the global stopping condition is
satisfied.
In each round, \texttt{BtMatchScheduler} selects a batch of
comparison triples $(i, j, q)$; model responses are generated and
cached; \texttt{JudgeService} dispatches the dual-call pairwise
comparison; the consolidated verdict is recorded to \texttt{ratings.db};
and $W^{(L)}$, $N^{(L)}$, BT estimates, and stopping state are
updated.
Each model is queried at most once per query; responses are reused
across all comparisons involving the same (query, model) pair.

\subsection{Match Scheduling}\label{subsec:scheduling}

\texttt{BtMatchScheduler} assigns a composite utility score to each
candidate comparison triple $(i, j, q, L)$ and selects the
highest-scoring batch.
The utility function combines a \textit{structural exploration} term
(prioritises unseen pairs) and an \textit{uncertainty resolution} term
(prioritises pairs with overlapping BT confidence intervals).
A mixing parameter $\lambda$ decays from exploration-dominated early
in the run to uncertainty-dominated once every pair has been compared
at least once.

The connectivity bootstrap phase guarantees that every model pair
$(i, j)$ at every leaf receives at least one comparison before
utility-driven scheduling begins.
Until full connectivity is achieved the structural exploration term
dominates ($\lambda \approx 1$); the BT model requires a connected
comparison graph for identifiability~\parencite{bradley1952rank}.
Bootstrapping all $\binom{M}{2}$ pairs is a stronger guarantee than
identifiability strictly requires (a spanning tree of $M-1$ edges
suffices), but it ensures that every pair receives at least one
unscheduled, order-naive comparison before utility-driven selection
introduces prioritisation bias.

Query selection within a scheduled pair prioritises queries by
descending cosine similarity to the leaf's vMF centroid
$\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_L$.
The formal specification of the utility function and decay schedule
are in Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology}.

\subsection{Stopping and Convergence States}

The stopping policy operates at three nested levels.
Table~\ref{tab:states} summarises the per-pair and per-leaf state

\textbf{Global stopping condition.}
The global condition is evaluated once per round as a query-weighted
converged fraction:
\[
f_{\text{conv}} =
\frac{\sum_{L \in \mathcal{C}} |Q_L|}{\sum_{L \in \mathcal{L}} |Q_L|},
\]
where $\mathcal{C} \subseteq \mathcal{L}$ is the set of leaves in
state \texttt{CONVERGED}.
Query-weighting prevents small boundary leaves from inflating the
convergence fraction.
The arena halts when $f_{\text{conv}} \geq f^*$ for $R_{\text{stable}}$
consecutive rounds; a hard escape valve terminates at $B_{\max}$ total
logical comparisons and marks the run \texttt{escaped = true}.

\subsection{Upward Score Aggregation to Anchor Domains}\label{subsec:aggregation}

After the arena run is complete, per-leaf verdict statistics are
propagated upward through the DAG to produce coherent rankings at
every level of the taxonomy: leaf, sub-domain, anchor domain, and root.

\textbf{Propagation via sufficient statistics.}
To produce a ranking at an ancestor node $A$, the aggregation
procedure pools the sufficient statistics of all descendant leaves
$\mathcal{D}(A)$ and refits a single Bradley--Terry model on the
pooled counts:
\[
W^{(A)} = \sum_{L \in \mathcal{D}(A)} W^{(L)},
\quad
N^{(A)} = \sum_{L \in \mathcal{D}(A)} N^{(L)}.
\]
A fresh MM optimisation on $(W^{(A)}, N^{(A)})$ produces the
ancestor-level parameter vector $\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(A)}$.

\textbf{Why leaf-level BT parameters must not be averaged.}
The BT model is identified only up to an additive constant per
connected comparison graph~\parencite{bradley1952rank}; the MM
algorithm applies an independent zero-sum normalisation at each leaf.
Averaging parameters across leaves combines differently-normalised
scales and produces a result with no probabilistic interpretation.
Pooling sufficient statistics and refitting produces a vector
identified on a single shared scale, with win probabilities
$P(m_i \succ m_j \mid A) = \sigma(\theta_i^{(A)} - \theta_j^{(A)})$
that are directly interpretable.

\textbf{Multi-assignment handling.}
The primary analysis uses the \textit{primary-path convention}: each
leaf contributes its sufficient statistics to the ancestor reached
via primary (splitter-generated) edges only, so that each verdict is
counted exactly once at every level of the hierarchy.
A fractional-weight sensitivity analysis is produced as a robustness
check in Chapter~\ref{ch:results}, in which a leaf $L$ with $k$
parent paths contributes $(1/k) \cdot W^{(L)}$ and
$(1/k) \cdot N^{(L)}$ to each ancestor.

\textbf{Ranking levels and the validation interface.}
The anchor-domain rankings, defined over $Q_D^{\text{adapted}}$,
form the primary validation interface: for each anchor $D$, the
Spearman rank correlation between $\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(D)}$ and
MMLU-Pro accuracy computed on the held-out query subset is reported
as a structural validity check.
The correlation is expected to be positive but not unity; divergence
is informative, as it surfaces queries where geometric proximity
diverges from editorial category membership.

\subsection{Reference-Informed Judge Design}\label{subsec:judge}

Every pairwise comparison in the arena is evaluated by an LLM judge
conditioned on domain-specific evaluation criteria generated at
taxonomy construction time.

\textbf{Reference-informed guideline generation.}
\texttt{GuidelineGenerator} produces a leaf rubric via a MapReduce
procedure over the leaf's construction query pool.

\textit{Map step.}
The construction queries assigned to leaf $L$ are partitioned into
small packs of $k$ queries (typically $k = 5$).
Each pack is presented to a strong LLM together with the MMLU-Pro
reference answers for those queries.
The model is prompted to identify quality principles that distinguish
correct from incorrect responses for queries of that type.
Each pack produces a local principle list.

\textit{Reduce step.}
The local principle lists are consolidated by a second LLM call that
de-duplicates, resolves conflicts, and produces a leaf-level rubric:
a ranked list of evaluation criteria specific to the semantic content
of leaf $L$.

\textit{Global template.}
A shared structural template covering presentation, tie handling, and
output format is prepended to every leaf rubric, ensuring that judge
calls across all leaves receive instructions in a consistent format.

\textit{Information separation invariant.}
Held-out evaluation queries and their answers never enter the
guideline generation procedure.
Only construction queries---whose ground-truth labels served as the
anchor initialisation signal---are presented to
\texttt{GuidelineGenerator}.
The rubric is informed by what correct responses look like in the
domain, but not by the specific queries on which models will be judged.

The generated rubrics are stored in \texttt{snapshots.db} alongside
the frozen DAG, so that every arena run on a given snapshot uses
identical guidelines regardless of when it is executed.

\textbf{Judge prompt structure.}
Each judge call receives a prompt structured as four sequential
blocks: (1)~domain guidelines, (2)~the query, (3)~Answer~A,
(4)~Answer~B.
The judge returns a structured verdict: \texttt{A}, \texttt{B},
or \texttt{TIE}.

\textbf{Dual-call consistency filtering.}
Each comparison $(m_i, m_j, q, L)$ is evaluated by two judge calls
with swapped presentation order.
A verdict is consistent when both calls agree on the winner; when
they disagree, the outcome is recorded as \texttt{TIE}.
This protocol converts positional disagreements into explicit ties
rather than noise in the BT sufficient statistics.

\textbf{Tie encoding.}
The \texttt{TIE} verdict is recorded as a fractional win of $0.5$
to each model---a pragmatic approximation within the standard BT
framework.
A proper Davidson model~\parencite{davidson1970ties} would introduce
a tie-propensity parameter per pair; this approximation is noted as
Limitation~5 in Section~\ref{sec:arch-limits}.

\textbf{Positioning relative to alternative judge designs.}
The reference-informed, domain-conditioned approach sits between two
alternatives: generic prompting (stable but domain-agnostic) and
alignment-aware designs such as MemAlign~\parencite{databricks2025memalign}
(stronger calibration but requiring external preference corpora).
The approach adopted here requires only the taxonomy and its
construction-set reference answers.

\textbf{Calibration caveat.}
Reference-informed conditioning improves the semantic relevance and
correctness-awareness of verdicts relative to generic prompting, but
calibration against human preference judgements is not structurally
guaranteed.
Where an overlap set between TaxoArena's held-out queries and publicly
available human preference annotations exists, empirical calibration
can be assessed; this is treated as optional future work.

\section{Persistence and Reproducibility}\label{sec:arch-infra}

TaxoArena partitions its persistent state across four primary SQLite
stores with distinct write lifecycles.
\texttt{embeddings\_cache.db} is written once before construction and
stores the full-dimensional Qwen3 MRL vectors as the single source of
truth for all downstream slice operations.
\texttt{mmlu\_pro\_dataset\_cache\_v2.db} stores the MMLU-Pro corpus
and the per-model ground-truth evaluations that serve as the
post-hoc validation oracle.
\texttt{snapshots.db} accumulates frozen DAG snapshots, each
identified by a \texttt{snapshotId} UUID; only frozen snapshots are
eligible as arena inputs.
\texttt{ratings.db} is append-only during an arena run and records every
verdict, the per-round BT estimates, and run-level metadata;
the \texttt{snapshotId} foreign key in every record allows the exact
DAG under which a verdict was produced to be recovered unambiguously.

Any result is fully specified by the reproducibility tuple
\texttt{(corpusVersion, snapshotId, runId)}: given these stores
and this tuple, every component of the pipeline can be re-executed
from scratch.
(The stores are plain SQLite files in WAL mode; per-run identifiers,
seeds, and realised call counts are additionally recorded in the
\texttt{manifest.json} emitted with each experiment run.)

\section{Architectural Limitations}\label{sec:arch-limits}

The following limitations are inherent to the architectural choices
described in this chapter.

\textbf{1. Seed dependence on MMLU-Pro editorial domains.}
The 14 anchor nodes are seeded from MMLU-Pro ground-truth labels and
rendered permanently immutable.
If the embedding space does not support 14 linearly separable
domain-level clusters, the anchor structure will preserve editorial
splits regardless of the geometry.
Cross-domain migration partially mitigates this at the query level,
but cannot relocate or merge anchors.
Results at the anchor-domain level should be interpreted as rankings
conditioned on the MMLU-Pro partitioning.

\textbf{2. Corpus-bounded discovery.}
The taxonomy is constructed from the 12{,}000 MMLU-Pro queries
available at construction time.
Sub-domains absent or sparsely represented in that corpus will not
appear as leaves.
Extending the system to a different corpus requires a full
reconstruction run; there is no incremental insertion mechanism.

\textbf{3. Embedding dependence.}
The entire geometric structure of the taxonomy is contingent on the
embedding model used at construction time.
If the embedding model changes, the snapshot is invalidated and full
reconstruction is required.
Arena results produced under different embedding models are not
directly comparable.

\textbf{4. Residual judge bias.}
The dual-call protocol mitigates positional bias but leaves two
biases architecturally unmitigated.
\textit{Verbosity bias}: LLM judges tend to prefer longer responses
independent of quality~\parencite{chen2024verbosity};
the rubric includes no explicit length-normalisation criterion.
\textit{Self-preference bias}: when the judge model coincides with an
evaluated model, verdicts may favour the judge's own response
style~\parencite{panickssery2024llm}; no judge-exclusion constraint
is enforced.
Their magnitude is bounded indirectly via the rank-stability analysis
across different judge temperatures; a direct comparison against
human preference annotations is identified as future work.

\textbf{5. Tie approximation and dual-call budget accounting.}
Ties are encoded as half-wins within the standard BT likelihood,
which does not include Davidson's tie-propensity
parameter~\parencite{davidson1970ties}; the approximation slightly
underestimates tie frequency.
Additionally, each logical comparison produces two API calls under the
dual-call design; budget reporting distinguishes
$B_{\text{logical}}$ (comparisons) from $B_{\text{API}} = 2 \cdot
B_{\text{logical}}$ (judge calls).
Convergence curves and efficiency comparisons are expressed in
$B_{\text{logical}}$ to remain comparable with single-call systems.

\textbf{6. Sparse-leaf risk at deep levels.}
A leaf with a small held-out query pool may exhaust its comparison
budget before the connectivity bootstrap achieves a fully connected
comparison graph, since each query can serve multiple pairs but the
number of pairs grows as $\binom{M}{2}$.
Such leaves will terminate in state \texttt{BUDGET\_EXHAUSTED};
their BT estimates enter the upward aggregation at full weight, which
may inflate uncertainty at the ancestor level.
The query-weighted convergence fraction $f_{\text{conv}}$ partially
absorbs this: sparse leaves contribute little weight to the global
condition.

\textbf{7. Absence of a production-ready router.}
The routing component exercised during the arena's pre-routing step
is not exposed as a standalone service.
This limitation is material only for deployment use cases; it does
not affect the validity of the offline evaluation reported in this
dissertation.

\section{Chapter Summary}

This chapter described TaxoArena as two coupled subsystems---taxonomy
construction and arena evaluation---connected through an immutable
DAG snapshot.
The taxonomy pipeline seeds 14 fixed MMLU-Pro anchors and induces
sub-domain structure beneath them through a six-phase iterative loop,
ending in a frozen, versioned snapshot; the full formal treatment of
this pipeline is given in Appendix~\ref{app:dag-formalism}.
The arena pipeline allocates comparisons adaptively, terminates under
a three-level state machine with a query-weighted global condition,
pools verdict sufficient statistics under the primary-path convention
for upward aggregation, and conditions each judgment on a
reference-informed leaf rubric generated via a MapReduce procedure
with dual-call consistency filtering.
Persistence is organised around the reproducibility tuple
\texttt{(corpusVersion, snapshotId, runId)}, and seven structural
limitations are catalogued: seed dependence, corpus-bounded discovery,
embedding dependence, residual judge bias, tie approximation and
dual-call budget accounting, sparse-leaf risk, and the absence of a
production-ready router.
The statistical formalism underlying the arena's Bradley--Terry
estimation, scheduling, and stopping policy is derived formally in
Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology}.
