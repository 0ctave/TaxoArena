\chapter{Literature Review}\label{ch:literature-review}




Five interdependent observations motivate the design of an automated,
domain-structured LLM evaluation framework.


First, existing evaluation benchmarks are structurally brittle: fixed
corpora saturate, contaminate, and fail to adapt to the capability
frontier.
Second, the semantic structure of a query corpus is recoverable from
embedding geometry, enabling taxonomy induction without editorial
supervision.
Third, automated pairwise judgments can be made reliable through bias
control and reference-informed domain conditioning, provided the
rubric-generation phase is distinguished from the answer-key-blind
evaluation phase.
Fourth, per-domain model rankings require a probabilistic pairwise
model with explicit uncertainty quantification and a sample-efficient
scheduler, and no existing system provides both within a
corpus-induced taxonomy.
Fifth, the downstream task of routing-aware model selection requires
per-domain capability profiles that no existing system produces
without presupposing a fixed taxonomy.




This chapter surveys the relevant work in the order of that dependency.
Section~\ref{sec:benchmarks} reviews LLM evaluation benchmarks,
establishing why fixed-corpus, scalar-ranking approaches fail to track
the capability frontier and why an evaluation structure induced from
query geometry is needed.
Section~\ref{sec:taxonomy-construction} covers hierarchical taxonomy
construction, surveying the geometric and statistical tools---vMF
distributions, Matryoshka embeddings, and the Dasgupta cost
function---that a query-level unsupervised taxonomy requires, and
identifying the gaps in prior classification and induction methods.
Section~\ref{sec:llm-as-a-judge} addresses LLM-as-a-judge reliability,
surveying the systematic biases that affect automated pairwise evaluation
and the domain calibration techniques that a per-domain judge design must
account for.
Section~\ref{sec:ranking-models} covers pairwise ranking models, from
the Elo baseline through the Bradley--Terry formulation to active ranking
theory, establishing the statistical and sample-complexity foundations
that a sample-efficient per-domain arena requires.
Section~\ref{sec:routing} closes the chapter by framing query routing
as the target use case: the downstream task for which per-domain
capability profiles are necessary and which existing routing systems
cannot fulfil without a principled profiling layer.




\section{LLM Evaluation Benchmarks}\label{sec:benchmarks}

\subsection*{Static Benchmarks and Their Limitations}

The first generation of Large Language Model (LLM) evaluation relied on
static instruments such as \textit{Massive Multitask Language Understanding}
(MMLU)~\parencite{hendrycks2021measuring} and the \textit{Holistic Evaluation
of Language Models} (HELM) framework~\parencite{liang2022holistic}.
MMLU uses over \num{15000} multiple-choice questions across 57 subjects to
probe professional and academic competence; at release, the best-performing
model (GPT-3, 175B) reached 43.9\% accuracy against an estimated human
expert ceiling of 89.8\%~\parencite{hendrycks2021measuring}.
HELM attempted a more multidimensional approach by evaluating 30 models
across 42 scenarios and seven metrics---accuracy, calibration, robustness,
fairness, bias, toxicity, and efficiency---making it the first large-scale
effort to resist the reduction of model quality to a single
number~\parencite{liang2022holistic}.

Both frameworks share a structural fragility: the evaluation corpus and its
categorical organisation are fixed at construction time.
This creates three compounding failure modes.
First, \textit{saturation}: as model capabilities advance, score
distributions compress toward the ceiling and the benchmark loses
discriminative power---frontier models now score between 97\% and 99\% on
MMLU, rendering it unable to rank them against each
other~\parencite{boubdir2024elo}.
Second, \textit{contamination}: benchmark questions circulate online and
enter training corpora; researchers found that ChatGPT and GPT-4 could guess
missing answer options in MMLU at rates of 52\% and 57\% respectively,
far above what genuine reasoning would predict~\parencite{deng2024contamination}.
Third, \textit{categorical brittleness}: editorially defined subject
categories cannot adapt to the capability space of new model generations,
leaving blind spots at boundaries and failing to capture cross-domain
dimensions that emerge only after the benchmark is fixed.
HELM's fixed scenario taxonomy, despite its breadth, inherits the same
limitation: its 42 scenarios were defined for the model generation of 2022
and do not evolve with the capability frontier~\parencite{liang2022holistic}.

\subsection*{From MMLU to MMLU-Pro}

The saturation and contamination of MMLU motivated a line of progressively
harder benchmarks.
\textit{BIG-Bench Hard} (BBH)~\parencite{suzgun2022bigbench} addressed
discriminability by extracting the 23 BIG-Bench tasks on which prior models
had not outperformed average human raters, covering multi-step arithmetic,
logical deduction, causal judgment, and temporal reasoning across 6,511
examples.
Crucially, BBH demonstrated that chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting enabled
PaLM to surpass human-rater performance on 10 of those 23 tasks---a result
that confirmed both the diagnostic value of the harder subset and the
inadequacy of the four-choice format for measuring genuine
reasoning~\parencite{suzgun2022bigbench}.

\textit{MMLU-Pro}~\parencite{wang2024mmlu} extends this logic directly to
the MMLU lineage.
It expands the answer set from four to ten options per question,
eliminates trivial and noisy items from the original MMLU, and integrates
reasoning-focused questions drawn from academic exams and textbooks across
14 domains---including mathematics, physics, chemistry, engineering, law,
and computer science---yielding \num{12000} rigorously curated
items~\parencite{wang2024mmlu}.
The effect on model scores is substantial: accuracy drops by 16\% to 33\%
relative to MMLU, and prompt sensitivity falls from 4--5\% to 2\%, indicating
that MMLU-Pro is more robust to surface variation and more discriminative of
underlying reasoning capability.

The problem of contamination, however, persists even in harder benchmarks
so long as questions are static.
\textit{LiveBench}~\parencite{white2024livebench} addresses this
structurally by refreshing its question pool on a rolling basis from
recently published sources, substantially reducing contamination risk
since questions are drawn from material published after common training
cutoffs.
This approach trades domain coverage for freshness, and its task set
remains narrower than MMLU-Pro's 14-domain structure.
The fundamental tension between depth of domain coverage (MMLU-Pro) and
resistance to contamination (LiveBench) motivates an alternative
approach: rather than fixing either the questions or the domains, a
framework that induces its evaluation structure dynamically from the
query corpus can avoid categorical brittleness, though its domain coverage
remains bounded by the diversity of the supplied corpus.


\subsection*{Pairwise Arena Evaluation Platforms}

A parallel line of work addressed a complementary limitation of static
benchmarks: the absence of human preference as an evaluation signal.
\textit{Chatbot Arena}~\parencite{chiang2024chatbotarena}, introduced by
Chiang et al.\ at UC Berkeley and LMSYS, is the canonical instance.
Users submit a free-form prompt, receive responses from two anonymous models
simultaneously, and vote for the preferred response; the platform has
collected over 240K votes across more than 50 models as of early
2024~\parencite{chiang2024chatbotarena}.
Pairwise comparisons are aggregated using a Bradley--Terry model, and
confidence intervals on the resulting coefficients are computed via the
sandwich robust standard error, providing statistically grounded rankings
with explicit uncertainty estimates.
To improve sample efficiency, Chatbot Arena employs an adaptive sampling
rule that allocates comparison budget proportionally to the reduction in
confidence interval size---the platform reports that this requires 54\% fewer
comparisons than random sampling to achieve the same precision on the win
matrix~\parencite{chiang2024chatbotarena}.

Recognising that a single global ranking conceals domain-specific
capability variation, Arena has more recently constructed per-category
leaderboards through an explicit taxonomy-building
pipeline~\parencite{arena2025categories}.
The methodology embeds prompts, clusters them at scale, uses an LLM to
generate candidate category names and definitions, and iterates with
human review toward two optimisation targets: coverage (at least 80\%
of prompts assigned to a named category) and boundary clarity (category
definitions remain mutually exclusive under re-labelling).
This pipeline shares the high-level goal of inducing a domain structure
from query data, but its design priorities---human interpretability and
leaderboard presentation---differ from those of statistical evaluation
rigour.

The differences, however, are substantive.
Arena's splitting criterion is a coverage percentage and a qualitative
boundary-clarity judgment, both requiring human-in-the-loop validation;
there is no information-theoretic objective function that makes the
resulting partition verifiably near-optimal.
Category nodes carry no statistical characterisation: there is no
probability model over the queries assigned to a category, so membership
decisions are hard and the coherence of a node cannot be measured.
Cluster labelling is delegated to an LLM prompted to propose
human-interpretable names, which optimises for presentation rather than
for geometric separability.
Finally, the process is shallow and manual: the Arena WebDev taxonomy
contains seven categories constructed over several weeks of iterative
refinement, whereas a principled recursive procedure would be required
to scale to hundreds of semantic sub-domains.
These limitations are not incidental---they reflect the fact that Arena's
taxonomy was designed for human-readable leaderboard presentation rather
than for statistical evaluation rigour.



\section{Hierarchical Taxonomy Construction}\label{sec:taxonomy-construction}

\subsection*{Supervised Hierarchical Text Classification}

Hierarchical text classification (HTC) organises a document collection
into a predefined taxonomy of classes, requiring a model to assign labels
at multiple levels of a tree or DAG simultaneously.
Early neural approaches treated the problem as flat multi-label
classification, ignoring the hierarchical dependencies between classes
and paying no structural penalty for errors at distant nodes.
The \textit{Hierarchy-Aware Global Model} (HiAGM)~\parencite{zhou2020hiagm}
addressed this by encoding the label hierarchy as a directed graph and
propagating label embeddings through structure encoders before fusing
them with text representations; two variants, HiAGM-LA (inductive,
attention-based) and HiAGM-TP (deductive, feature-propagation), both
show consistent gains over flat baselines on RCV1-v2 and WoS.
\textit{HiTIN}~\parencite{zhu2023hitin} subsequently converted the label
hierarchy into a \textit{coding tree} derived from structural entropy,
then applied a tree-isomorphism-aware graph network to capture
multi-granularity label co-occurrences without manual feature engineering.

These methods share a critical dependency: the hierarchy itself must be
supplied as supervision.
The label tree for RCV1 was constructed by Reuters editors; the WoS
hierarchy was constructed by domain librarians.
Neither process is reproducible, scalable to new domains, or verifiable
in terms of coherence---the granularity, balance, and semantic
separability of the nodes are editorial artefacts.
This dependency defines the scope boundary for classification-based
approaches: they assume an already-coherent taxonomy and learn to
navigate it, but provide no method for constructing or validating
that taxonomy in the first place.

\subsection*{Unsupervised Taxonomy Induction}

A distinct line of work removes the editorial dependency by constructing
the taxonomy directly from corpus statistics.
\textit{TaxoGen}~\parencite{zhang2018taxogen} builds a topic taxonomy
in a recursive top-down fashion using two coupled modules: an
\textit{adaptive spherical clustering} module that assigns terms to
levels by jointly measuring their similarity to a sub-topic centroid
and their discriminative concentration relative to sibling topics,
and a \textit{local embedding} module that retrains term embeddings on
the sub-corpus of each node before splitting, enhancing discriminative
power at lower levels where global embeddings are too coarse.
TaxoGen operates on term-level embeddings derived from word2vec and
evaluates coherence using Davies--Bouldin index and human judgement,
without a formal objective function governing the splitting decision.

\textit{CoRel}~\parencite{huang2020corel} extends this paradigm to the
seed-guided setting, where a user provides a partial skeleton taxonomy
and CoRel completes it by learning a relation-transferring module that
propagates the user's intended hierarchical relation along embedding
paths and a concept-learning module that enriches each node with a
cluster of coherent representative terms.
While CoRel substantially improves taxonomic precision over unsupervised
methods, it introduces a seed dependency that partially re-introduces the
human-editorial bottleneck.

TaxoArena shares the seed-guided starting point but differs in kind:
the MMLU-Pro labels initialise anchor membership and are thereafter
superseded by geometric evidence (migration, splitting, and topology
operations), whereas CoRel's relation-transferring module permanently
encodes the user-supplied skeleton as a structural prior throughout
the construction process.

Both approaches operate at the \textit{term level}---each node is a
cluster of words or phrases extracted from a corpus.
Applying them at the \textit{query level}---where each document is a
full natural-language question and each node should characterise the
distributional geometry of its member queries---requires a different
statistical model, a different splitting criterion, and a different
coherence measure, none of which are provided by the TaxoGen or CoRel
frameworks.

\subsection*{Spherical Embeddings and vMF Clustering}

When text is encoded by a neural model and $\ell_2$-normalised to the
unit hypersphere, cosine similarity becomes the natural inner product,
and the von Mises--Fisher (vMF) distribution becomes the canonical
probability model for directional data~\parencite{banerjee2005vmf}.
The vMF density for a unit vector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{S}^{d-1}$,
with mean direction $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and concentration $\kappa \geq 0$, is
\begin{equation}
    p(\mathbf{x};\boldsymbol{\mu},\kappa) =
    C_d(\kappa)\exp\!\bigl(\kappa\,\boldsymbol{\mu}^\top\mathbf{x}\bigr),
    \label{eq:vmf}
\end{equation}
where $C_d(\kappa) = \kappa^{d/2-1}\bigl[(2\pi)^{d/2}
I_{d/2-1}(\kappa)\bigr]^{-1}$
and $I_\nu$ denotes the modified Bessel function of the first kind.
Banerjee et al.\ showed that fitting a mixture of vMF distributions via
EM is equivalent to minimising a Bregman divergence on the unit sphere,
yielding the \textit{movMF} algorithm whose M-step update for $\kappa$
requires solving
\begin{equation}
    A_d(\kappa) =
    \frac{I_{d/2}(\kappa)}{I_{d/2-1}(\kappa)} = \bar{r},
    \label{eq:kappa}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{r} = \|\sum_i r_i \mathbf{x}_i\| / \sum_i r_i$ is the
mean resultant length~\parencite{banerjee2005vmf}.
Because $A_d^{-1}$ has no closed form, Hornik and Gr\"{u}n derived a
high-order approximation that is accurate to within
$O(\kappa^{-4})$~\parencite{hornik2014vmf}, resolving the numerical
instability of the fixed-point iteration for the very high concentration
parameters ($\kappa \gg 1$) that arise in high-dimensional
embedding spaces.


\subsection*{Overlapping, Polyhierarchical, and Cross-Domain Taxonomies}

The taxonomy models reviewed above---TaxoGen, CoRel, and the vMF node
model---all assume that each item belongs to exactly one node at each
level of the hierarchy.
This assumption is convenient for statistical fitting but is not a
structural property of real query corpora: a query about the
thermodynamics of a chemical reaction is legitimately a member of both
a physics-oriented and a chemistry-oriented sub-domain, and forcing an
exclusive assignment discards this evidence.
\textit{Soft} or \textit{overlapping} hierarchical clustering methods
address this by allowing an item to hold non-trivial membership in
more than one cluster, typically through a fuzzy or probabilistic
membership function rather than a hard partition~\parencite{fuzzyhc1969}.
Applied to a directed hierarchy, this relaxation produces a
\textit{polyhierarchy}: a taxonomy in which a node may have more
than one parent, so that the structure is a directed acyclic graph
(DAG) rather than a tree~\parencite{polyhierarchy2006}.
Polyhierarchies are well established in knowledge organisation---for
instance, a single subject heading in a controlled vocabulary
routinely appears under more than one broader term---but their use in
corpus-induced, embedding-driven taxonomy construction remains sparse:
existing induction methods (TaxoGen, CoRel) produce trees, and the
DAG structure in taxonomy-induction pipelines is typically imposed
post hoc rather than learned from overlap evidence in the embedding
space.

A related but distinct mechanism is \textit{cross-domain bridging}:
rather than softening membership everywhere, a bridging step
identifies a small number of structurally overlapping node pairs
across otherwise separate branches of the hierarchy and inserts an
explicit edge (or a shared child) connecting them, while leaving the
remainder of the hierarchy as a tree.
This is a more conservative departure from strict tree structure than
global soft clustering, since it introduces polyhierarchy only where
the embedding geometry provides positive evidence of genuine overlap
(e.g. high cross-cluster cosine similarity coupled with low
within-cluster purity loss), rather than allowing every item a
non-trivial membership everywhere.
TaxoArena's construction pipeline combines both ideas but keeps them
statistically separate: query assignment during vMF/NiW fitting
remains exclusive (Section~\ref{sec:taxonomy-construction}, node
model), so that the concentration and covariance estimates at each
node stay clean, while polyhierarchy is introduced afterward, at the
topology-optimisation stage, through two distinct bridge-insertion
mechanisms---residual-routing bridges within a branch and
coherence-gated cross-domain bridges between branches---each admitted
only under an explicit coherence and distinctness gate.
This design is formalised in Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology}
(topology optimisation) and distinguishes TaxoArena from both the
fully-soft polyhierarchy literature, where membership is fuzzy
throughout, and from the strictly exclusive tree literature (TaxoGen,
CoRel, HiAGM, HiTIN), where no cross-branch edge can be represented
at all.


\textit{Matryoshka Representation Learning} (MRL)~\parencite{kusupati2022mrl}
achieves this by optimising a single embedding function jointly across a
schedule of nested prefix dimensions
$\mathcal{M} = \{m_1, m_2, \ldots, m_L\}$ with $m_1 < m_2 < \ldots < m_L = d$,
using a weighted sum of task losses evaluated at each prefix
length~\parencite{kusupati2022mrl}.
The key property is \textit{coarse-to-fine information packing}: because
the prefix is optimised at every scale simultaneously, the most
discriminative semantic directions are concentrated in the leading
coordinates, making the prefix at any $m' < m_L$ a coherent lower-dimensional
representation rather than a truncated artefact.
This coarse-to-fine packing property is the operational basis for the
depth-dimension schedule used in TaxoArena's taxonomy construction.
At each recursion depth $\ell$, the DAG node model is fitted on the
prefix $\mathbf{x}_{1:m_\ell}$ rather than on the full embedding:
shallow nodes use a narrow prefix because domain-level distinctions
are geometrically separable in low dimension, while deep nodes use a
wider prefix because fine-grained sub-topic distinctions require
higher-dimensional resolution to remain geometrically coherent.
MRL guarantees that each prefix is a faithful lower-dimensional
representation, so the splitting decision at depth $\ell$ is not
confounded by the dimensional truncation artefacts that would arise
from na\"ively slicing a standard embedding.

\subsection*{Cost-Based Hierarchical Splitting}

Classical hierarchical clustering algorithms---single linkage, average
linkage, complete linkage---are specified procedurally rather than in
terms of an objective function, making it impossible to reason about
whether a given tree is near-optimal or to compare competing algorithms
on principled grounds~\parencite{dasgupta2016cost}.
Dasgupta introduced a cost function over binary trees that resolves this:
given a weighted similarity graph $G = (V, E, w)$, the cost penalises
partitions that separate similar item pairs high in the tree---cutting a
high-weight edge at the root incurs a penalty proportional to $n$, whereas
cutting it near a leaf incurs a penalty close to $1$~\parencite{dasgupta2016cost}.
Dasgupta proved three properties of this cost function.
First, on planted-partition data it behaves optimally in expectation,
recovering the ground-truth clusters because any non-laminar top split
incurs excess expected cost proportional to the inter-cluster separation.
Second, the optimal tree is NP-hard to compute exactly.
Third, the greedy top-down heuristic---repeatedly finding an
approximate sparsest cut and recursing---achieves an
$O(\alpha_n \log n)$ approximation ratio, where $\alpha_n$ is the
approximation factor of the sparsest-cut oracle~\parencite{dasgupta2016cost}.

Critically for taxonomy induction, the Dasgupta cost provides a
formally grounded objective for evaluating competing hierarchies: a
proposed split is geometrically motivated when it yields a measurable
decrease in the cost function, in contrast to heuristic thresholds
such as TaxoGen's Davies--Bouldin criterion, which lack a
tree-global optimality interpretation.
The Dasgupta framework does not itself define a local stopping rule;
the normalised cost-delta threshold used as a split-acceptance
criterion in TaxoArena is an adaptation introduced in this thesis
and formalised in Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology}.


\subsection*{Evaluation Metrics for Hierarchies}

Evaluating a taxonomy requires metrics that respect the hierarchical
structure: a classification that assigns a query to a sibling node
should incur a smaller penalty than one that assigns it to a
taxonomically distant node.
Flat metrics such as accuracy or macro-$F_1$ are blind to this
structure, producing the same penalty regardless of how far the
predicted label is from the true one~\parencite{kosmopoulos2015hmetrics}.
Kosmopoulos et al.\ conducted a unified analysis of hierarchical
evaluation measures, showing that the most commonly used ones can be
decomposed into a generic \textit{hierarchical precision} (hP) and
\textit{hierarchical recall} (hR) based on the overlap between the
predicted and true \textit{augmented label sets}---the label and all
its ancestors~\parencite{kosmopoulos2015hmetrics}.
The resulting $h\!F_\beta$ measure is consistent with human judgements
of classification quality across three large text-classification
benchmarks, and is parameterisation-agnostic in the sense that it
applies equally to tree and DAG taxonomies without modification.

\bigskip
\noindent
The literature reviewed in this section identifies three requirements
for the taxonomy construction component of an LLM evaluation framework:
a node model that statistically characterises cluster geometry (vMF);
a splitting criterion with a formal objective function rather than a
heuristic threshold (Dasgupta cost); and an embedding space in which
both coarse and fine-grained distinctions are geometrically resolvable
(MRL).
The supervised classification literature (HiAGM, HiTIN) requires an
editorial hierarchy as input and therefore cannot serve as a construction
method.
The unsupervised induction literature (TaxoGen, CoRel) operates at the
term level, lacks a formal splitting criterion, and does not produce a
statistical node model.
These gaps jointly define the requirements for a query-level unsupervised
taxonomy construction method: a statistical node model over directional
embeddings, a formally grounded splitting criterion, and a multi-scale
embedding space.



\section{LLM-as-a-Judge and Reliability}\label{sec:llm-as-a-judge}

As human evaluation becomes a scalability bottleneck, the LLM-as-a-judge
paradigm has emerged as the dominant approach to automated assessment of
model outputs~\parencite{zheng2023judging}.
This approach leverages the high-level reasoning capabilities of frontier
models to evaluate the semantic quality of candidate responses; its
reliability properties---and the conditions under which they hold---are
therefore directly relevant to any evaluation framework that delegates
comparison to an automated judge.

\subsection*{The Judge Paradigm}

The foundational distinction in automated judging is between
\textit{pointwise} and \textit{pairwise} evaluation.
Pointwise evaluation asks a judge model to assign an absolute score to a
single response; pairwise evaluation presents two responses simultaneously
and asks for a preference judgment~\parencite{zheng2023judging}.
Zheng et al.\ introduced MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena to benchmark these
paradigms against human preference, finding that pairwise evaluation
achieves higher agreement with human raters and is more robust to
prompt-formatting variation---a direct consequence of the relative nature
of the task reducing the cognitive load of score calibration.
The superior calibration of pairwise judgments is the primary empirical
justification for preferring pairwise arena evaluation over pointwise
scoring in automated benchmarks.

\subsection*{Systematic Biases}

A significant challenge in pairwise automated evaluation is the
presence of systematic biases that distort preferences independently
of response quality.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Positional bias.}
    Judges tend to favour the response presented first in the prompt,
    independently of content~\parencite{zheng2024judging}.
    Zheng et al.\ found that GPT-4 agreed with itself on only 85\% of
    cases when the position of two responses was swapped, implying that
    up to 15\% of pairwise outcomes are position-driven rather than
    quality-driven.

    \item \textbf{Verbosity bias.}
    Models consistently assign higher scores to longer responses, even
    when the additional content adds no reasoning
    depth~\parencite{li2024verbosity, chen2024verbosity}.
    This confound is particularly acute in knowledge-intensive QA,
    where a model can pad a correct but terse answer with
    confident-sounding elaboration to win a preference judgment it
    should not have won.

    \item \textbf{Self-preference bias.}
    LLMs exhibit a tendency to favour outputs stylistically similar to
    their own training distribution~\parencite{wataoka2025selfpreference}.
    Recent evidence characterises this as a familiarity bias: a model
    assigns lower perplexity to outputs close to its own distribution and
    translates that fluency signal into a preference judgment, effectively
    rewarding self-similarity rather than correctness.
\end{itemize}

Mitigation strategies include position-swapping with consistency checks,
length-normalised scoring, and group-based polling to average out
idiosyncratic preferences~\parencite{zheng2024judging}.
Positional bias is the most tractable of the three: a single
swap-and-consistency-check protocol identifies and discards
position-driven verdicts at low cost.
Verbosity bias is partially tractable through domain-specific prompt
engineering that makes evaluation criteria explicit, though the
effectiveness of this mitigation is an empirical question in any
given domain.
Self-preference bias remains the least tractable: it is structural
rather than prompt-addressable, and its magnitude varies with the
distance between the judging model's training distribution and
those of the evaluated models.

\subsection*{Domain Calibration}

A judge prompted with a generic instruction to ``prefer the better
answer'' applies implicit quality criteria drawn from its training
distribution, which may not align with the domain-specific standards
relevant to the query~\parencite{databricks2025memalign}.
This misalignment is not merely a calibration error in the statistical
sense; it changes the semantic content of the preference judgment.
In mathematical reasoning, for instance, a judge without domain
conditioning will prefer a fluent but incorrect derivation over a
terse but correct one, conflating presentation quality with
mathematical validity.

Two recent approaches address this.
\textit{MemAlign}~\parencite{databricks2025memalign} aligns synthetic
judges with domain-specific standards using a dual-memory system: a
semantic memory that stores general evaluation principles and an episodic
memory that stores specific edge-case examples, together calibrating the
judge against as few as 30--50 human gold labels without weight updates.
\textit{Judge-wise Reinforcement Learning}~\parencite{huang2025thinkj}
rewards the judge for providing explicit chain-of-thought justifications
that are consistent with the final preference, making the evaluation
process auditable and reducing the proportion of preference reversals
attributable to surface features.
Both approaches confirm that domain-specific judge conditioning
substantially improves alignment with human expert ratings, at the cost
of requiring either labelled examples or a reasoning-capable judge model.
A weaker but taxonomy-unsupervised form of domain conditioning---generating
evaluation guidelines from query samples rather than from gold
annotations---has not been systematically studied in the literature.

This represents a gap between the full MemAlign pipeline (which
requires human preference labels) and the setting that arises in
TaxoArena: the judge-generation phase has access to MMLU-Pro
reference answers from the construction set but not to human
preference annotations, while the arena evaluation phase has access
to neither---a \textit{reference-informed, answer-key-blind} design
that has not been systematically studied in the literature.


\bigskip
\noindent
The three biases and the domain-calibration requirement jointly define
the constraints on the judge design in any pairwise arena framework.
Positional bias motivates presentation-order randomisation and
consistency filtering.
Verbosity bias motivates explicit per-domain evaluation criteria that
do not reward length---though the assumption that prompt-level criteria
suppress this bias must be validated empirically rather than asserted.
Self-preference bias is acknowledged as a residual structural limitation
that cannot be eliminated without rotating judge models.
This motivates a taxonomy-unsupervised, reference-conditioned
guideline-generation mechanism whose design is specified in Chapter~\ref{ch:system-architecture}
to occupy a position between keyword-based centroid
descriptions (no semantic calibration) and MemAlign (requires gold
labels); whether it achieves adequate calibration is an empirical question rather than a structural guarantee.

The pairwise preference observations produced by such a judge---one
verdict per comparison, with ties represented as half-wins to each
model, following the tie-as-half-win convention introduced for
paired-comparison models by Davidson~\parencite{davidson1970}---constitute
the observations consumed by the ranking models reviewed in the
following section.



\section{Pairwise Ranking Models}\label{sec:ranking-models}

This section reviews the statistical foundations of pairwise ranking,
establishes the limitations of the dominant scalar approach, and
contextualises active scheduling within the theoretical literature on
sample-efficient ranking.

\subsection*{Elo and Its Limitations}

The foundational framework for comparative evaluation is the \textit{Elo
rating system}~\parencite{elo1978rating}.
Developed for zero-sum games like chess, it assumes that an agent's performance
is a random variable centred around a latent skill level $R$.
The probability $E_A$ of agent $A$ defeating agent $B$ follows a logistic
distribution~\parencite{ostasiewicz2022evaluation}:
\begin{equation}
    E_A = \frac{1}{1 + 10^{(R_B - R_A)/400}}
    \label{eq:elo-expected}
\end{equation}
While Elo is robust for tracking stable skill levels over time, it suffers from
several weaknesses in LLM evaluation contexts.
It assumes static skill between matches and does not explicitly model uncertainty,
leading to ranking instability in populations with high variance or sparse
interaction data~\parencite{boubdir2024elo}.
Furthermore, Elo ratings are fundamentally transitive, meaning they assume that
if $A \succ B$ and $B \succ C$, then $A \succ C$; research indicates that LLMs
frequently exhibit non-transitive preferences, rendering linear Elo leaderboards
statistically misleading~\parencite{boubdir2024elo}.

\subsection*{Bradley--Terry and Bayesian Extensions}

The \textit{Bradley--Terry} (BT) model provides the formal probabilistic
link for pairwise preferences, modelling the merit of each model as a
latent parameter $\lambda_i > 0$~\parencite{bradley1952rank}.
The probability that model $i$ is preferred over model $j$ is
\begin{equation}
    P(i \succ j) = \frac{\lambda_i}{\lambda_i + \lambda_j}
    = \sigma(\theta_i - \theta_j),
    \label{eq:bt}
\end{equation}
where $\theta_i = \log \lambda_i$ and $\sigma$ is the logistic function.
The log-likelihood is strictly concave in $(\theta_i)$, and the MLE
$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ is obtained via iterative re-weighted least
squares (IRLS) or the MM algorithm~\parencite{bradley1952rank}.
The asymptotic covariance of $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ is the inverse of
the Fisher information matrix, yielding per-parameter standard errors
$\hat{\sigma}_i$.
A necessary condition for identifiability is that the comparison graph---the
graph in which each node is a model and each edge represents at least one
observed comparison---is connected; if the graph decomposes into two or more
components, BT parameters are identified only up to an additive constant
per component and cross-component comparisons cannot be
inferred~\parencite{bradley1952rank}.

This identifiability requirement, often treated as a constraint to be
engineered around in global arenas, becomes a structural advantage of
the per-domain decomposition in TaxoArena: by restricting the
comparison graph to the small, semantically coherent query pool of a
single leaf, the bootstrap phase needs only $\binom{M}{2}$ initial
comparisons---one per pair---to guarantee connectivity, a number that
is tractable even for leaves with modest query populations.
Graph connectivity is necessary but not sufficient for a finite
unregularised MLE to exist: complete separation (one model
winning every comparison against another) prevents convergence of
the MM algorithm; the regularisation strategy used to handle this
case is specified in Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology}.

This is a non-trivial requirement in per-domain arenas where the query
pool at a leaf may be small and not all model pairs are guaranteed to
receive at least one comparison.

More advanced systems, such as Microsoft's
\textit{TrueSkill}~\parencite{herbrich2007trueskill}, represent skill as a
Gaussian distribution $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, tracking both mean skill
and uncertainty.
By calculating the information gain of each match, Bayesian models converge
faster than standard Elo~\parencite{joshy2024openskill}.
Modern implementations such as the \textit{OpenSkill} library use the
Weng--Lin Bayesian approximation~\parencite{weng2011bayesian}; its
Plackett--Luce model is particularly effective for multi-model scenarios
where margin-of-victory information can accelerate convergence toward a
stable ranking~\parencite{joshy2024openskill}.

\subsection*{Active Ranking and Sample Complexity}

The na\"ive strategy for recovering a full ranking of $M$ models using pairwise
comparisons is round-robin evaluation, requiring $\binom{M}{2}$ comparison
pairs per leaf.
\textit{Active ranking} addresses this by selecting, at each step, the
comparison most likely to reduce global ranking uncertainty.

The theoretical basis for this approach was established by Jamieson and
Nowak~\parencite{jamieson2011activeranking}, who showed that if $n$ items can
be embedded in a $d$-dimensional metric space consistent with the ranking, then
a sequentially adaptive algorithm recovers the full ranking using only
$O(d \log n)$ pairwise comparisons on average---an exponential reduction over
the $\Theta(n^2)$ required by non-adaptive strategies.
The same work proves that \textit{random} (non-adaptive) sampling requires
almost all $\binom{n}{2}$ comparisons to identify the ranking with
reasonable probability, establishing a fundamental separation between adaptive
and non-adaptive query selection.
The metric-space embedding assumption underlying this result---that model
capabilities can be represented as points in a low-dimensional Euclidean
space whose order is consistent with the true ranking---is an idealisation.
LLM capabilities are likely not perfectly embeddable in a fixed
low-dimensional metric space; restricting the ranking problem to a
semantically coherent sub-domain reduces the effective intrinsic
dimensionality and makes the metric-space assumption more defensible,
which is a requirement for the theoretical guarantee to be practically
meaningful.

For the stochastic setting---where comparison outcomes are noisy Bernoulli
random variables, as in an LLM judge---Heckel et al.~\parencite{heckel2019activeranking}
derive finite-sample bounds under the Bradley--Terry model directly.
Their main result characterises the number of comparisons required to
distinguish two adjacent models in the ranking with error probability $\delta$:
the sample complexity scales as $O(\Delta^{-2} \log(M/\delta))$, where
$\Delta$ is the minimum separation between consecutive BT parameters.
Two consequences follow.
First, pairs whose BT parameters are close together require
disproportionately many comparisons; a uniform budget allocation is
therefore inefficient.
Second, a confidence-interval-based allocation rule---scheduling
comparisons for the pair with the largest overlap in BT confidence
intervals---is near-optimal in the sense that it achieves the
$O(\Delta^{-2} \log(M/\delta))$ bound without knowledge of
$\Delta$~\parencite{heckel2019activeranking}.
Heckel et al.\ further establish that imposing a parametric model (BTL,
Thurstone) offers at most $O(\log n)$ improvement over non-parametric
adaptive strategies for stochastic comparisons, indicating that the
primary practical advantage of the BT model lies in its interpretability
and its natural provision of a convergence criterion via standard errors,
rather than in any asymptotic sample-complexity gain.

\subsection*{Multidimensional Ranking and Intransitivity}

A significant structural barrier in model evaluation is the presence of cyclic
dominance, the ``Rock-Paper-Scissors'' effect, which occurs when models have
specialised capabilities that excel in certain domains but fail in others,
violating the transitivity assumption of scalar
models~\parencite{balduzzi2018melo}.
To model these interactions, current research utilises vectorised approaches.
\textit{mELO}~\parencite{balduzzi2018melo} utilises Hodge Decomposition to
separate model interactions into a transitive (scalar) component and a cyclic
(skew-symmetric) component, allowing more accurate win-rate predictions where
models exhibit diverse, niche strategies.
The \textit{Blade-Chest model}~\parencite{chen2016bladechest} infers a
$d$-dimensional representation for each model, modelling ``offence'' and
``defence'' separately; by learning a dataset-specific metric space, it can
represent any pairwise stochastic preference relation~\parencite{liu2024probabilistic}.
Both approaches confirm that a single global ranking cannot faithfully
represent the capability profile of models that excel in some domains
and fail in others, and that direct interaction modelling can lead to more accurate predictions of pairwise outcomes.

\bigskip
\noindent
Taken together, the ranking literature identifies three requirements for
an arena evaluation framework: a probabilistic pairwise model with explicit
uncertainty quantification and a connected comparison graph as a
precondition for identifiability; an adaptive scheduler that concentrates
comparisons on the hardest-to-distinguish pairs, achieving near-optimal
sample complexity without knowledge of the true parameter separations; and
a per-domain decomposition that makes the transitivity assumption locally
defensible and reduces the effective dimensionality required by the
Jamieson--Nowak metric-space bound.
These requirements directly motivate the routing use case examined in
Section~\ref{sec:routing}.


\section{Query Routing as the Target Use Case}\label{sec:routing}

This section frames query routing as the downstream task that per-domain
capability profiles are designed to enable.
It reviews the evidence that global rankings are insufficient for routing,
surveys the learned routing systems that would consume per-domain profiles,
and identifies the gap---the absence of a principled method for producing
those profiles---that motivates the contribution of this thesis.

\subsection*{The Routing Problem in Agentic Systems}

In multi-model agentic systems, a query is dispatched to one of several
candidate models based on an estimate of which model will perform best
on that query.
The optimal dispatch policy requires knowledge of each model's
domain-conditional performance: if model $A$ outperforms model $B$ on
mathematical reasoning but underperforms on legal analysis, a global
ranking that averages across domains will systematically mis-route
queries at both extremes.

This observation motivates the concept of \textit{domain-centric
evaluation}~\parencite{raju2024constructing}, in which a model's
performance is disaggregated by subject matter rather than reported
as a single aggregate score.
Raju et al.\ demonstrate this on chat-assistant evaluation: by
constructing high-separability domain-specific evaluation sets---using
manual seed curation combined with embedding-based clustering---they
show that model rankings are not stable across domains, and that a
router informed by per-domain scores substantially reduces
response-quality variance relative to global-rank routing.
Meimandi and collaborators extend this to the agentic setting,
framing the problem as \textit{positioning}: finding the domain
sub-space in which each model occupies the highest-ranked
position~\parencite{meimandi2025positioning}.
Both bodies of work share a common dependency: they require that
per-domain scores already exist and that the domain taxonomy is
either given or constructed by hand, which limits reproducibility
and scalability.

\subsection*{Learned Routing from Preference Data}

The cost-quality trade-off in model selection has motivated a parallel
line of work on \textit{learned routers} that predict, prior to
generation, which model will produce the preferred response.
\textit{FrugalGPT}~\parencite{chen2023frugalgpt} operationalises this
as a cascade: it queries an ordered sequence of models of increasing
capability and stops when a learned confidence estimator judges the
current response to be reliable, achieving reductions in inference
cost of up to 98\% at matched accuracy on several NLP tasks by
reserving the strongest model for the fraction of queries that require it.

\textit{RouteLLM}~\parencite{ong2024routellm} addresses the same
cost-quality trade-off through a trained binary routing function rather
than a cascade.
It trains four router architectures---a similarity-weighted Bradley--Terry
model, a matrix factorisation model, a BERT classifier, and a causal LLM
classifier---on 80K pairwise preference judgements from Chatbot Arena,
learning to predict the probability that the stronger model wins on a
given query.
The similarity-weighted BT router is the most directly interpretable
of the four architectures: it computes query-similarity-weighted BT
coefficients at inference time and uses the resulting win probability
as the routing signal, making its inputs---per-domain BT scores over
a representative query set---explicit rather than latent.
A critical finding is that routers trained solely on the global Arena
preference distribution generalise poorly to domain-specific benchmarks
such as MMLU and GSM8K, with performance close to random; significant
improvement requires augmenting the training data with in-domain
preference labels~\parencite{ong2024routellm}.
This result is directly relevant to the motivation for per-domain
profiling: a globally trained BT model does not encode the
domain-conditional capability variation that accurate routing requires.

\subsection*{Embedding-Based Model Selection}

A complementary approach bypasses the preference-data requirement
by representing models as embeddings in a learned query-performance
space.
\textit{EmbedLLM}~\parencite{zhuang2024embedllm} represents each model
as a compact feature vector derived from its performance on a set of
representative prompts, and routes new queries to the model whose
embedding is geometrically closest to the query in a learned
correctness space.
\textit{UniRoute}~\parencite{somerstep2025uniroute} generalises this
by learning a joint query-model embedding space in which routing reduces
to a dot-product operation, enabling sub-millisecond dispatch at
inference time.
Both systems require an initialisation step in which each model is
characterised over a representative query set; the quality of the
resulting profile determines the upper bound on routing accuracy.

A limitation shared by FrugalGPT, RouteLLM, EmbedLLM, and UniRoute
is that the domain structure over which models are profiled is either
absent (global ranking) or predefined by the benchmark used to construct
the training set.
None of these systems provide a method for inductively deriving the
domain taxonomy from the query corpus itself, nor do they produce
statistically characterised per-domain profiles that can be validated
for coherence independently of routing accuracy.
These two absences define the gap between the learned routing literature
and the problem addressed in this thesis.

\section*{Synthesis: Five Gaps and One Compound Problem}

The five bodies of literature reviewed in this chapter converge on a
single compound problem.
Static benchmarks fail because their corpus and categorical structure
are fixed at construction time, making them unable to track the
capability frontier~(\S\ref{sec:benchmarks}).
Taxonomy construction methods from NLP either require an editorial
hierarchy as supervision or operate at the term level without a
formal splitting criterion, and neither provides a statistical node
model~(\S\ref{sec:taxonomy-construction}).
LLM judges exhibit systematic positional, verbosity, and
self-preference biases that are partially---but not fully---addressable
through prompt engineering, and domain calibration without gold labels
remains an open problem~(\S\ref{sec:llm-as-a-judge}).
Pairwise ranking models provide the statistical foundations for
per-domain arenas, but their sample-efficiency guarantees depend on a
metric-space assumption that is only locally defensible within
semantically coherent sub-domains~(\S\ref{sec:ranking-models}).
Learned routing systems require per-domain capability profiles that
no existing system can produce without presupposing a fixed
taxonomy~(\S\ref{sec:routing}).

TaxoArena addresses this compound problem through two integrated
contributions: a label-bootstrapped, corpus-induced geometric
taxonomy---seeded from MMLU-Pro ground-truth labels as a warm start
but refined entirely by embedding geometry, and admitting
polyhierarchy only through gated bridge insertion rather than through
unconstrained soft membership---that produces the coherent sub-domains
required by both the ranking model and the domain judge; and a
reference-informed, answer-key-blind arena that produces the
per-domain profiles required by routing.
An active, uncertainty-driven scheduler keeps the judge-call cost of
the arena tractable within each leaf's comparison graph, but is
treated as a supporting mechanism for the second contribution rather
than as an independent contribution in its own right (see
Section~\ref{sec:research-goal}).
The system design is specified in Chapter~\ref{ch:system-architecture};
the formal foundations are derived in Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology}.
