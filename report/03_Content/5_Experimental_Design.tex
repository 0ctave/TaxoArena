\section{Implementation Environment}\label{sec:exp-env}

The pipeline is implemented in Kotlin on Spring Boot, backed by
SQLite in WAL mode for the three persistent databases described in
Section~\ref{sec:arch-infra}.
Embeddings are produced by Qwen3-Embedding served locally via Ollama;
the judge model is Mistral-Large-3, accessed through an Azure OpenAI
endpoint.
All experiments are executed on [CPU/GPU spec, RAM, OS --- to be
finalised at submission].
Reproducibility follows the tuple convention of
Section~\ref{sec:arch-infra}: every run is seeded from
$\{42, 137, 2048\}$, and the canonical \texttt{TaxonomyConfig} values
are \texttt{splitThreshold} $= 0.02$ (equivalently reported as 80 in
the pre-normalised internal scale), \texttt{minClusterSize} $= 15$,
\texttt{separationEpsilon} $= 0.08$, routing temperature $\tau = 2.0$,
\texttt{assignmentGap} $\delta_{\text{assign}} = 0.2$, and EMA
smoothing $\alpha_{\text{EMA}} = 0.5$ for maturity tracking.
Exact commit SHA and environment lockfile are recorded in
\texttt{REPRODUCIBILITY.md} and cited at submission.

\section{Datasets}\label{sec:exp-datasets}

\subsection{Primary Corpus: MMLU-Pro}

The primary corpus is MMLU-Pro~\parencite{wang2024mmlupro}, 12,000
questions across 14 domains with human-verified reference answers.
MMLU-Pro is used for two roles simultaneously: it supplies the
ground-truth domain labels that seed the 14 anchor nodes
(Section~\ref{sec:meth-migration}), and it supplies the per-domain
accuracy scores used as the external validation signal
(Section~\ref{sec:meth-validation}).
For the results reported in this thesis, taxonomy construction and
arena evaluation are run on the \textbf{Math} sub-corpus of MMLU-Pro:
1,079 queries after deduplication, chosen as the primary experimental
domain because it has among the largest per-domain query counts and
the clearest ground-truth answer format, minimising label noise in
the accuracy computation.

\subsection{Secondary Corpus: AG News}

AG News~\parencite{zhang2015agnews}, a four-class topic classification
corpus of news headlines, is used as a pipeline sanity check: it has
no MMLU-Pro-style domain structure and no reference-answer format
compatible with the reference-informed judge design, so it is not
used for anchor seeding or arena evaluation.
Its role is limited to verifying that the embedding, vMF fitting, and
splitting stages of the taxonomy pipeline execute correctly and
produce a geometrically coherent DAG on a corpus with known,
independently-labelled topic structure, as a check against
implementation bugs that MMLU-Pro's larger and noisier label space
might mask.

\section{Domain Selection and Split Protocol}\label{sec:exp-split}

\subsection{Domain Scope}

The experiments in this thesis are scoped to the Math domain of
MMLU-Pro rather than all 14 domains simultaneously.
This scoping decision reflects a compute-budget trade-off: a
full 14-anchor run multiplies the arena's judge-call budget by
roughly an order of magnitude for the same per-leaf statistical
power, and the core research questions (taxonomy quality, RQ1;
migration effect, RQ2; scheduling efficiency, RQ3) are each
answerable within a single, well-populated domain.
Generalisation across domains is identified as future work in
Section~\ref{sec:future-work}.

\subsection{Stratified Split}

The 1,079-query Math sub-corpus is split 70/30 into a construction
set (755 queries, used for taxonomy induction, migration scoring, and
reference-informed guideline generation) and an arena set (324
queries, reserved exclusively for held-out routing and pairwise
evaluation).
The split is stratified by the finest available sub-topic tag in the
MMLU-Pro metadata, ensuring that both partitions are proportionally
representative before any geometric restructuring occurs, and is
fixed across all three random seeds to isolate taxonomy-construction
variance from split variance.

The 30\% arena allocation is chosen so that the smallest leaf in the
canonical run receives approximately 12 held-out queries.
At 16 evaluated models, this leaf presents $\binom{16}{2} = 120$
model pairs; with the connectivity bootstrap requiring at least one
comparison per pair and each of the 12 queries eligible for multiple
pairs, this establishes an upper bound of 1,440 judge calls
(120 pairs $\times$ 12 queries) at that leaf before any adaptive
scheduling logic is invoked, which is the binding constraint on
choosing a smaller arena fraction.
A sensitivity row repeats the canonical run at a 40\% arena split to
verify that headline Spearman $\rho$ results are not an artefact of
the specific split ratio.

\section{Reference-Informed Judge Generation Configuration}\label{sec:exp-judge}

The judge rubric generation procedure (Section~\ref{subsec:judge})
is instantiated as follows.
Representative-query packs are drawn from each leaf's construction
pool at pack size $k = 5$; a leaf with fewer than 5 construction
queries uses its full pool as a single pack.
The map-step LLM and the reduce-step LLM are the same underlying
model as the arena judge, Mistral-Large-3, to avoid introducing a
second model's stylistic biases into the rubric; this choice is
revisited as a limitation in Section~\ref{sec:arch-limits}.
The global structural template---covering presentation order, tie
handling, and output format---is fixed across all leaves and is
listed in full in Appendix~\ref{app:judge-template}.
Guideline generation is a one-time cost per frozen snapshot: rubrics
are computed once at snapshot-freeze time and reused for every
subsequent arena run against that snapshot, regardless of which
models are being compared.

\section{Arena Evaluation Configuration}\label{sec:exp-arena}

\subsection{Evaluated Models}

The canonical arena evaluates 16 models spanning open-weight and
proprietary families at comparable parameter-count tiers [full model
list and provider details in Appendix~\ref{app:models}].
The canonical construction run yields 17 leaves under the Math
anchor; all 17 are included in the arena's held-out routing target
set.

\subsection{Scheduling and Budget}

The per-pair comparison budget is $B_{ij} = 20$ (\texttt{budget\_pair}
$= 20$), and the arena runs for a maximum of 10 scheduling rounds
(\texttt{maxRounds} $= 10$) before the global escape valve is
consulted.
Each logical comparison is realised as two judge calls under the
dual-call, position-reversed protocol of Section~\ref{subsec:judge},
so the canonical arena's theoretical judge-call ceiling is
$2 \times 20 \times \binom{16}{2} \times 17 = 81{,}600$ calls if every
pair at every leaf exhausted its full budget.
This ceiling is not the expected utilisation: with 324 held-out
queries distributed across 17 leaves, the mean leaf receives
approximately 19 queries, and the connectivity bootstrap alone
(one comparison per one of $\binom{16}{2} = 120$ pairs) already
approaches or exceeds a leaf's available query pool before
utility-driven scheduling or per-pair budget exhaustion becomes the
binding constraint.
The 81,600 figure is reported only as an upper bound for provisioning
API rate limits; the expected realised call count, together with the
per-leaf query-pool-to-pair-count ratio and its effect on how many
leaves reach \texttt{BUDGET\_EXHAUSTED} versus \texttt{CONVERGED}, is
reported directly in Chapter~\ref{ch:results}.

\subsection{Random Scheduler Ablation}

A random scheduler ablation replaces the composite-utility pair and
query selection of Section~\ref{sec:meth-scheduling} with uniformly
random pair and query selection, while retaining the same per-pair
and per-leaf budgets and the same three-level stopping policy.
This ablation isolates the marginal contribution of adaptive
scheduling by comparing, at matched final judge-call counts, the
total match count, the achieved Spearman $\rho$ against MMLU-Pro
accuracy, and the number of rounds to convergence between the
adaptive and random-scheduler conditions.
An exhaustive round-robin condition (every pair against every routed
query, no stopping) is deliberately not included as a primary
ablation: at the canonical budget it is computationally prohibitive
across all 17 leaves, and it is reported only if the implementation
status permits a reduced-scale run, consistent with
Chapter~\ref{ch:introduction}.

\subsection{Verbosity Bias Probe}

Fifty judge matches are sampled uniformly from the canonical arena
run and re-judged with response position swapped, independent of the
standard dual-call protocol's own position randomisation, to produce
an out-of-protocol audit sample.
The position-flip rate (fraction of re-judged matches whose verdict
changes under the swap) and a per-model verbosity delta
$\Delta_{\text{verbosity}}$ (difference in win rate attributable to
response length after controlling for BT score) are reported.
This probe is specifically designed to confirm or quantify an anomaly
observed in preliminary runs (\texttt{arenabenchmark.log}) in which
one evaluated model's verdicts appeared to correlate with response
length independent of content quality; the probe is a targeted
diagnostic rather than a general-purpose bias correction.

simpler unsupervised alternatives on identical embeddings.

\section{Metrics and Statistical Protocol}\label{sec:exp-metrics}

\subsection{Taxonomy Quality Metrics}

Nine metrics characterise the induced DAG independent of arena
evaluation: hierarchical F1 (hP, hR, H-F1) and Edge F1 against the
MMLU-Pro-derived gold DAG structure where applicable to the supervised
baselines; Triplet Accuracy; Dendrogram Purity and Weighted Leaf
Purity; Spherical Silhouette; Total Dasgupta Cost; Normalised Sackin
Index (tree balance); and Routing Expected Calibration Error (ECE) of
the trickle-routing softmax against ground-truth domain membership.

\subsection{Arena Quality Metrics}

Per-leaf and per-anchor BT scores are reported with their Fisher
information standard errors; Spearman $\rho$ against MMLU-Pro
accuracy is the headline validation statistic
(Section~\ref{sec:meth-validation}); $\Delta_{\text{verbosity}}$ and
judge Cohen's $\kappa$ (inter-call agreement under the dual-call
protocol) quantify judge reliability; total match count and matches
per leaf quantify budget efficiency.

\subsection{Aggregation and Significance Testing}

All headline numbers are reported as mean $\pm$ standard deviation
over the three seeds $\{42, 137, 2048\}$.
The primary significance test is a Wilcoxon signed-rank test on
per-leaf Spearman $\rho$, comparing the adaptive scheduler against
the random-scheduler ablation at matched judge-call budgets; this
non-parametric paired test is chosen over a paired $t$-test because
per-leaf $\rho$ values are bounded in $[-1,1]$ and are not assumed
normally distributed across the small number of leaves.
A pre-specified threshold of $\rho > 0.7$ (strong positive rank
correlation) is adopted before data collection, consistent with the
success criterion stated in Chapter~\ref{ch:introduction}, as the
minimum bar for declaring the arena ranking a useful proxy for
MMLU-Pro accuracy at the anchor-domain level; results below this
threshold are reported and discussed but not characterised as
validating the arena's discriminative power.
Bootstrap confidence intervals (2,000 resamples, percentile method)
are reported alongside every Spearman $\rho$ and $\Delta\rho$
estimate to quantify sampling uncertainty given the finite held-out
query pool per leaf.
Resampling is performed at the query level within domain: each
bootstrap resample draws held-out queries with replacement from
$Q_{D_k}^{\text{adapted}}$ (or $Q_{D_k}^{\text{canonical}}$ for Track
A), and all verdicts associated with a sampled query are carried into
the resample together, preserving the within-query dependence
structure noted as a methodological assumption in
Section~\ref{sec:meth-assumptions} rather than treating individual
verdicts as independent resampling units.

\section{Chapter Summary}

This chapter fixed, before data collection, the mapping from research
question to experimental condition and metric (Table~\ref{tab:rq-map}),
then specified the concrete implementation environment; dataset
preparation (MMLU-Pro Math sub-corpus as primary, AG News as a
pipeline sanity check); the domain-scoping decision and 70/30
stratified split protocol with its query-budget rationale; the
canonical taxonomy construction hyperparameters and eight-experiment
ablation matrix, including the reporting-sensitivity distinction for
A3--A4; the reference-informed judge generation configuration; the
arena's model roster, scheduling budget, the four arena-level
conditions C1--C4 that isolate the contribution of geometric
partitioning and reference-informed judging (Table~\ref{tab:arena-conditions}),
the information-separation protocol formalising the answer-key-blind
design (Table~\ref{tab:info-separation}), and the random-scheduler
and verbosity-bias diagnostic conditions; five taxonomy-structure
baseline comparisons spanning supervised HTC and unsupervised
clustering; and the metrics and statistical protocol---including the
pre-specified $\rho > 0.7$ validation threshold, the query-level
bootstrap resampling procedure, and the caveat on the theoretical
judge-call ceiling---used to interpret the results reported in
Chapter~\ref{ch:results}.
