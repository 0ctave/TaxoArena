\chapter{Numerical Methods}\label{app:numerics}

This appendix collects the numerical-stability derivations referenced
from Chapters~\ref{ch:introduction} and~\ref{ch:methodology} and from
Appendix~\ref{app:dag-formalism}.
Each subsection documents what the implementation actually computes
(\texttt{StatisticsUtils}, \texttt{TaxonomyFitter},
\texttt{BtMatchScheduler}, \texttt{BtMmFitter}), not an idealised
variant.

\section{Log-Bessel Evaluation at High Dimension}\label{sec:num-bessel}

The vMF normaliser $C_d(\kappa) = \kappa^{d/2-1} / \left((2\pi)^{d/2}
I_{d/2-1}(\kappa)\right)$ requires the modified Bessel function
$I_\nu(\kappa)$ at order $\nu = d/2 - 1 = 127$ for $d = 256$, far
outside the range where direct series evaluation is stable.
The implementation evaluates $\log I_\nu(\kappa)$ by the uniform
(Debye-type) asymptotic approximation for large order: with
$z = \kappa / \nu$, $t = \sqrt{1 + z^2}$, and
$\eta = t + \log\!\left(z / (1 + t)\right)$,
\[
\log I_\nu(\kappa)
\approx \nu\,\eta
- \tfrac{1}{2}\log\!\left(2\pi\nu\right)
- \tfrac{1}{4}\log\!\left(1 + z^2\right),
\]
which is the leading term of the uniform asymptotic expansion of
$I_\nu(\nu z)$ and is accurate to well below the tolerance of any
routing or fitting decision for the $\nu$ used here.
For near-zero argument ($\kappa \leq 10^{-4}$), the small-argument
limit $\log I_\nu(\kappa) \approx \nu \log(\kappa/2) -
\log\Gamma(\nu + 1)$ is used instead, with $\log\Gamma$ evaluated by
the Lanczos approximation; in the same regime the vMF log-normaliser
falls back to the uniform-distribution surface term
$\log\Gamma(d/2) - (d/2)\log\pi - \log 2$.
The Bessel ratio required by the concentration MLE
(Appendix~\ref{app:dag-formalism}),
\[
A_d(\kappa) = \frac{I_{d/2}(\kappa)}{I_{d/2-1}(\kappa)}
= \exp\!\left(\log I_{d/2}(\kappa) - \log I_{d/2-1}(\kappa)\right),
\]
is computed as the exponential of the difference of two log-Bessel
evaluations and clamped to $[0, 1]$, its analytic range.
The same ratio's derivative,
$A_d'(\kappa) = 1 - A_d(\kappa)^2 - \tfrac{d-1}{\kappa} A_d(\kappa)$,
drives the Newton--Raphson refinement of
$\hat{\kappa}$ described in Appendix~\ref{app:dag-formalism}.

\section{NiW Posterior Update and Student-$t$ Predictive}\label{sec:num-niw}

Each node stores a diagonal Normal-inverse-Wishart posterior over its
member embeddings (weighted by the trickle weights $w_i$ with
effective count $n = \sum_i w_i$).
The prior is
$m_0$ = the normalised mean of the parents' vMF centroids (the first
basis vector at the root), pseudocount $\kappa_0 = 1$, degrees of
freedom $\nu_0 = d + 2$, and scale
$\boldsymbol{\Lambda}_0 = \lambda_0 \mathbf{I}_d$ with $\lambda_0 =
1/(\kappa_{\text{parent}}\, d)$, where $\kappa_{\text{parent}}$ is
the parent-anchored prior concentration.
With weighted sample mean
$\bar{\mathbf{x}} = \tfrac{1}{n}\sum_i w_i \mathbf{x}_i$, the
conjugate updates are
\[
\kappa_N = \kappa_0 + n,
\qquad
\nu_N = \nu_0 + n,
\qquad
m_N = \frac{\kappa_0 m_0 + n \bar{\mathbf{x}}}{\kappa_N},
\]
and, per coordinate $j$ (the implementation stores only the diagonal
of the scale matrix),
\[
\Lambda_{N,j}
= \lambda_0
+ \sum_i w_i \left(x_{ij} - \bar{x}_j\right)^2
+ \frac{\kappa_0 n}{\kappa_N}\left(\bar{x}_j - m_{0,j}\right)^2 .
\]
The corresponding posterior predictive is a multivariate Student-$t$
with $\nu_N - d + 1$ degrees of freedom, location $m_N$, and (diagonal)
scale $\boldsymbol{\Lambda}_N (\kappa_N + 1) / (\kappa_N (\nu_N - d +
1))$.
In the current implementation this predictive is not used for
routing---routing is vMF-based
(Appendix~\ref{app:dag-formalism})---and the stored posterior serves
diagnostic roles, notably the leaf log-semantic volume
$\sum_j \log \Lambda_{N,j}$ tracked by the convergence stabiliser.

\section{KDE Bandwidth for the Scheduler Valley Weight}\label{sec:num-kde}

The match scheduler's density-aware pair weight rewards pairs whose
score midpoint falls in a low-density region of the current
Bradley--Terry score distribution.
Given the $n$ current scores with sample standard deviation
$\hat{\sigma}$, the kernel bandwidth follows Silverman's rule of
thumb for Gaussian kernels,
\[
h = 1.06\, \hat{\sigma}\, n^{-1/5},
\]
floored at $10^{-4}$; the density at a pair's midpoint
$m = (s_i + s_j)/2$ is the Gaussian kernel density estimate
\[
\hat{f}(m) = \frac{1}{n h \sqrt{2\pi}}
\sum_{k=1}^{n} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(s_k - m)^2}{2h^2}\right),
\]
normalised by the maximum density attained at any score point, and
the valley weight is $1 - \hat{f}(m)/\hat{f}_{\max}$, floored at
$0.1$ so no pair is fully suppressed.
Two gap-based guards precede the density term: a pair whose score gap
exceeds $4(\hat{\sigma}_i + \hat{\sigma}_j)$ is suppressed entirely
(already decided), and a pair below the noise floor
$0.15(\hat{\sigma}_i + \hat{\sigma}_j)$ is linearly ramped
(indistinguishable at current precision).

\section{Observed Fisher Information for BT Standard Errors}\label{sec:num-fisher}

The Bradley--Terry log-likelihood over pairs with comparison counts
$n_{ij}$ and win probabilities
$p_{ij} = \sigma(s_i - s_j)$ has observed information matrix
\[
\mathcal{I}(\mathbf{s})
= \sum_{i < j} n_{ij}\, p_{ij} (1 - p_{ij})\,
(\mathbf{e}_i - \mathbf{e}_j)(\mathbf{e}_i - \mathbf{e}_j)^\top,
\]
i.e.\ $\mathcal{I}_{ii} = \sum_{j \neq i} n_{ij} p_{ij}(1-p_{ij})$
and $\mathcal{I}_{ij} = -\,n_{ij} p_{ij}(1-p_{ij})$, evaluated at the
MM fixed point $\hat{\mathbf{s}}$.
$\mathcal{I}$ is singular with rank deficiency one (row sums vanish),
reflecting the additive-constant non-identifiability that the
zero-sum constraint $\sum_i s_i = 0$ removes.
The implementation resolves the singularity by inverting the
rank-completed matrix $\mathcal{I} + \mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top$
(Gauss--Jordan with partial pivoting) and recovering the
constrained covariance as
\[
\operatorname{Var}(\hat{s}_i)
= \left[(\mathcal{I} + \mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top)^{-1}\right]_{ii}
- \frac{1}{K},
\]
where $K$ is the number of models; adding
$\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top$ is equivalent to imposing the sum-to-zero
constraint, and subtracting $1/K$ removes the artificial variance the
completion introduces along the constrained direction.
If the completed matrix is numerically singular, the implementation
falls back to the diagonal approximation
$\operatorname{Var}(\hat{s}_i) \approx 1/\mathcal{I}_{ii}$.
Reported standard errors are
$\hat{\sigma}_i = \sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(\hat{s}_i)}$, floored at
$0.01$; because comparisons sharing a query are treated as
independent, these standard errors slightly underestimate the true
uncertainty (Section~\ref{sec:meth-assumptions}).
