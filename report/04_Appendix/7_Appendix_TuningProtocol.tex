\chapter{Taxonomy Tuning Protocol and Ablations}\label{app:tuning-protocol}

This appendix details the mechanics behind the canonical hyperparameters
and construction ablations referenced in Chapter~\ref{ch:experimental-design}
(see Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams} for the selected values).

\section{Hyperparameter Selection Protocol}\label{sec:exp-tuning-protocol}

The canonical values in Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams} are not
hand-picked; they are the output of an L9($3^4$) orthogonal-array
design over four factors---\texttt{minClusterSize},
\texttt{routingSoftmaxTau}, \texttt{assignmentCosineGap}, and
\texttt{deltaAssign}---each tested at three levels, run against a
Pareto selection procedure (\texttt{tools/tuning/pareto.py}) driven by
the harness \texttt{tools/tuning/tuning\_harness.py}.
The L9 levels are \texttt{minClusterSize} $\in \{50, 75, 100\}$,
\texttt{routingSoftmaxTau} $\in \{1.0, 1.5, 2.0\}$,
\texttt{assignmentCosineGap} $\in \{0.10, 0.15, 0.20\}$, and
\texttt{deltaAssign} $\in \{0.5, 1.0, 2.0\}$.

Configuration selection proceeds in three steps. Every candidate is
first checked against the eight hard gates in
Table~\ref{tab:pareto-gates}; a single failure excludes it. The same
candidate is then scored against the six soft gates in
Table~\ref{tab:pareto-gates}, whose outcome is recorded per candidate
but does not exclude it. All hard-gate survivors are then ranked by
Pareto dominance count over twelve coherence and calibration
objectives---five maximised (Weighted Leaf Purity, Dendrogram Purity,
Spherical Silhouette, $\Delta\rho_{\text{total}}$,
canonical--adapted Jaccard) and seven minimised (Total Dasgupta Cost,
Routing ECE, Brier score, no-match rate, small-leaf fraction,
$\kappa$-shrinkage mean, selected-node starved-leaf
fraction)---with raw Top-1 accuracy as the tiebreaker. Small-leaf
fraction, starved-leaf fraction, and canonical--adapted Jaccard
appear in both layers: the gate sets a hard floor, and the dominance
ranking additionally credits margin beyond it.

The dynamic-temperature exponent $\gamma$ is \textbf{not} part of the
L9 design: because $\tau_{\text{eff}} = \tau\kappa^{\gamma}$ couples
non-linearly to $\kappa$, it is ablated as a standalone factor at
$\{0, 0.5, 1.0\}$ (A11) rather than orthogonally. No reserved-arena-set
label, accuracy, or judge verdict is used by either the hard or soft
gates at any point in the L9/Pareto procedure; this is the mechanism
by which the tuning/test-leakage boundary is enforced in practice, not
merely asserted.

Each ablation is run across all three seeds and reported as mean
$\pm$ std following the statistical protocol of
Section~\ref{sec:exp-metrics}. A3 and A4 (marked $\dagger$) are not
system ablations in the same sense as the others: they vary the
\textit{metric computation formula} used to score a fixed,
already-constructed taxonomy, rather than a system parameter that
changes the taxonomy itself. They are retained as
\textit{reporting-sensitivity analyses}, testing whether headline
quality conclusions are an artefact of a specific metric definition.
A6 is a joint sweep of the two nat-margin secondary-assignment gates
because they jointly determine multi-leaf assignment and bridge
admission; a one-factor-at-a-time sweep would miss their interaction.
A9, A10, and A11 isolate the \texttt{DAG\_MAX}-specific mechanisms
(soft-membership refitting, two-source bridging, and the dynamic
routing temperature) whose marginal contribution to taxonomy quality
and bridge coverage needs to be demonstrated, not merely described.

\section{Optional Secondary Diagnostics}\label{sec:appendix-optional-diagnostics}

The following conditions are not part of the minimum defensible
package (Table~\ref{tab:arena-conditions}) and are reported only
where run, as secondary diagnostics.

\subsection{Random Scheduler Ablation (C4)}

Because the scheduler is not a headline contribution
(\S\ref{sec:exp-rqmap}), the random scheduler ablation (C4) replaces
the composite-utility pair and query selection of
Section~\ref{sec:meth-scheduling} with uniformly random pair and
query selection, retaining the same per-pair and per-leaf budgets and
the same three-level stopping policy. It is compared against the
adaptive scheduler at matched final judge-call counts on total match
count, achieved Kendall $\tau$/pairwise-winner accuracy against
MMLU-Pro, and rounds to convergence. The budget-fidelity curve
($\rho$ or $\tau$ against cumulative judge calls) and a Wilcoxon
signed-rank test on per-leaf Kendall $\tau$ (chosen over a paired
$t$-test since per-leaf rank-agreement values are bounded and not
assumed normally distributed) compare the adaptive scheduler against
this ablation at matched judge-call budgets. An exhaustive
round-robin condition is not included: it is computationally
prohibitive at the 12-model arena scope and even less justified given
the scheduler is not a headline contribution.

\subsection{Verbosity Bias Probe}

Fifty judge matches are sampled uniformly from the canonical arena
run and re-judged with response position swapped, independent of the
standard dual-call protocol's own position randomisation, producing
an out-of-protocol audit sample. The position-flip rate (fraction of
re-judged matches whose verdict changes under the swap) and a
per-model verbosity delta $\Delta_{\text{verbosity}}$ (win-rate
difference attributable to response length after controlling for BT
score) are reported. This probe targets an anomaly observed in
preliminary runs (\texttt{arenabenchmark.log}) in which one evaluated
model's verdicts appeared to correlate with response length
independent of content quality; it is a targeted diagnostic, not a
general-purpose bias correction.
